When She Says Something Doesn't Feel Right

A woman's intuition about her own body is the most underutilized diagnostic tool in medicine. Your job is to listen, validate, and act.

scroll ↓
The Visit

Day 4 — "I Don't Feel Right"

You arrive at her apartment for a routine postpartum doula visit. Baby is feeding well. Partner is present. House is calm. She should be the picture of a good recovery.

But something is off. She's quiet. She's usually animated. She's sitting very still. When you ask how she's feeling, she pauses for too long and says:

"I don't know. I just... don't feel right."

Most people would say: "That's normal. You just had a baby." You don't say that. You sit down. You ask:

"Tell me what 'not right' feels like."

She thinks. "My heart feels fast. I'm sweating but I'm cold. And when I stood up to get the baby, everything went gray for a second."

The Assessment

The Numbers Behind "Not Right"

You open your doula kit. Blood pressure cuff. Thermometer. Pulse ox. Watch.

None of these numbers in isolation is catastrophic. Heart rate 118 — tachycardia, but not extreme. BP 96/58 — low, but she might "just run low." Temp 99.1 — borderline, not fever. Respiratory rate 24 — elevated but not alarming.

Together, they tell a story: Tachycardia + borderline hypotension + elevated respiratory rate + cold/clammy extremities + positional lightheadedness = compensated shock until proven otherwise.

You check the pad. She changed it 45 minutes ago. It's soaked.

What You Do Next

You don't diagnose. You don't panic. You say: "I'd like to call your provider right now. Your heart rate is faster than I'd expect, your blood pressure is low, and you're bleeding heavier than I'd like to see on day 4. I want them to know." You call. You relay the vitals. They say: "Bring her in."

"She told everyone she didn't feel right. They told her she was being dramatic. The doula was the only one who asked what 'not right' meant."
The Outcome

Subinvolution Caught at Day 4

At the hospital, ultrasound showed retained placental fragments — subinvolution of the placental site. The fragments were preventing her uterus from fully contracting. She was slowly hemorrhaging from a site that wouldn't clot. Hemoglobin: 7.2 (normal >12).

She went to the OR for a D&C. She received 2 units of packed red blood cells. She went home 24 hours later.

Without that visit — without you asking "what does 'not right' feel like?" — she would have continued bleeding. The body can only compensate for so long. By day 5 or 6, the compensation would have failed. Her blood pressure would have crashed. She would have been another ER admission, another ICU transfer, another near-miss — or another death.

The Lesson: Listen to Her

Women know their bodies. When a woman says "something doesn't feel right," that is a clinical finding. It deserves the same weight as a vital sign abnormality — because it usually IS a vital sign abnormality that hasn't been measured yet.

Your job:

1. Listen. Don't dismiss. Don't normalize. Don't say "that's just postpartum."

2. Ask. "Tell me what 'not right' feels like." Make it specific. Make it concrete.

3. Measure. Take the vitals. Put numbers to the feeling.

4. Call. Report findings to the provider. You're not diagnosing — you're communicating.

5. Act. Transport if necessary. Stay with her. Document everything.

She said five words: "I just don't feel right." You were the only one who heard them as a warning instead of a complaint. That is the doula difference.
