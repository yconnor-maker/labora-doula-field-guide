Intermediate Tier · Module 12

Lactation Challenges

Supply worries, latch pain, engorgement, mastitis, tongue-tie, pumping logistics, and the enormous emotional weight of breastfeeding struggles. The doula's role is not to be a lactation consultant — it's to normalize difficulty, provide practical support, know when to refer, and support her feeding choice without judgment.

Scope of PracticeYou are not an IBCLC. You do not diagnose tongue-tie, assess milk transfer, prescribe galactagogues, or provide clinical lactation counseling. You support, normalize, troubleshoot basics, refer to lactation professionals, and validate her experience.

What You'll Learn

  • Recognize and help with engorgement, latch difficulty, and nipple pain
  • Assess adequacy of intake (diaper output, weight gain)
  • Understand true low supply vs. perceived low supply
  • Know when to refer to an IBCLC (urgently)
  • Support formula feeding without stigma
  • Recognize mastitis and when it becomes an abscess

The First 72 Hours

Days 1–3 set the trajectory. What happens in the first few days determines breastfeeding success or failure more than anything else:

  • Day 1: Colostrum. Thick, yellow, small volumes (5–7 mL per feed). Newborn stomach is marble-sized. Frequent feeding (8–12× per 24 hours) is expected.
  • Days 2–3: Colostrum transitioning. Increased volume. Baby may cluster-feed intensely — this stimulates milk production, not a sign of inadequacy.
  • Days 3–5: Milk "comes in." Sudden breast fullness, warmth, heaviness. If severe: engorgement. Frequent feeding + hand expression + warm compresses BEFORE feed + cold compresses AFTER feed.
From the PhysicianThe number one reason I see women stop breastfeeding before they wanted to: they interpret normal newborn behavior as a sign of failure. Cluster feeding at 48 hours is not "your milk isn't enough." It's the baby's way of placing the order for the milk supply. The baby is programming the production. When someone tells a new mother on day 2 that "the baby must be hungry because you're not making enough," that person has just started the cascade toward supplementation, decreased stimulation, and actual supply decline. Your job as a doula: protect those first 72 hours.

Common Challenges

Engorgement

Days 3–5 milk transition with severe fullness, firmness, warmth, and latch difficulty (the breast is too firm for the baby to compress). Management: warm compress before feeding, hand express to soften areola (reverse pressure softening), cold compresses after feeding, frequent feeding, avoid skipping feeds or waiting for "fullness."

Latch Difficulty

Most common reason for early breastfeeding cessation. Signs of poor latch: pain throughout the entire feed (not just initial 10–30 seconds), clicking sounds, baby slipping off repeatedly, no audible swallowing (listen for soft "kuh" sounds), misshapen nipple after feed (flattened, creased, lipstick-shaped). IBCLC referral within 24–48 hours of persistent difficulty — do not wait.

Low Supply — Real vs. Perceived

The most common fear — and most often unfounded. Adequate intake indicators:

  • 6+ wet diapers per day by day 4
  • 3+ stools per day in the first month (transitioning from meconium to yellow, seedy)
  • Regaining birth weight by 2 weeks
  • Audible swallowing during feeds
  • Baby satisfied after feeds — releasing breast voluntarily, relaxed hands

True low supply causes: insufficient glandular tissue (hypoplasia), hormonal dysfunction (thyroid, PCOS, Sheehan syndrome), retained placenta, prior breast surgery (reduction especially), medications (pseudoephedrine, combined oral contraceptives), significant hemorrhage at delivery.

Mastitis

Breast tissue infection, usually unilateral. Signs: red, warm, firm wedge-shaped area, fever ≥101°F, flu-like symptoms. Continue breastfeeding — the milk is safe. Emptying the breast is therapeutic. If no improvement in 12–24 hours: antibiotics needed. If a firm, fluctuant area develops (you can feel a "pocket"): possible abscess — needs imaging and drainage.

Tongue-Tie (Ankyloglossia)

Restricted lingual frenulum limiting tongue mobility. Can cause poor latch, nipple pain, inadequate milk transfer, slow weight gain. Diagnosis by provider (IBCLC, pediatric dentist, ENT). Treatment: frenotomy (snipping). Controversial — overdiagnosis is real. Not every frenulum requires release.

Supporting Formula Feeding

Formula is not failure. Many women choose formula, need formula, or combine breast and bottle. Your role:

What You Can Do

  • → Never say "breast is best" as a pressure tool — it creates guilt without support
  • → Help with paced bottle feeding technique (upright position, pausing)
  • → Know WIC covers formula — connect if needed
  • → Never comment on someone else's feeding choice — at the park, online, or in your birth work
  • → "You are feeding your baby. You are a good mother." — this is enough

When to Refer — Urgently

⚠ IBCLC / Provider Referral Needed

  • Baby not back to birth weight by 2 weeks
  • Fewer than 6 wet diapers per day after day 4
  • Latch pain that persists beyond the first 30 seconds throughout the feed
  • Cracked, bleeding, or blistered nipples
  • Baby falling asleep immediately at breast without audible swallowing
  • Mastitis symptoms (unilateral red wedge + fever)
  • Jaundice deepening (increasingly yellow skin) — may indicate inadequate intake

Test Your Understanding

1. In the first 24 hours, a newborn's stomach is approximately the size of a:

The day-1 stomach holds only 5–7 mL — marble-sized. Colostrum is perfectly matched in volume. Frequent feeding (8–12×/day) is normal and expected.

2. Cluster feeding at 48 hours indicates:

Cluster feeding is the baby programming the milk supply. Frequent feeding stimulates prolactin and "places the order" for milk production. This is NOT a sign of inadequacy.

3. When should you refer to an IBCLC?

Early IBCLC referral (24–48 hours) prevents the cascade: persistent pain → decreased feeding → decreased stimulation → actual supply decline → supplementation → weaning.

4. Adequate intake by day 4 includes:

By day 4: 6+ wet diapers, 3+ stools daily. Baby should regain birth weight by 2 weeks. Fewer diapers or continued weight loss = IBCLC referral.

5. Your client is crying because she decided to switch to formula. The best response is:

She doesn't need data, encouragement to try harder, or comparison. She needs validation. Formula is not failure. Her decision is her own. Your job is to support it fully.
