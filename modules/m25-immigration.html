Immigration Barriers

Public charge fears, emergency Medicaid, language access, and serving undocumented clients. Immigration status should never determine whether a mother receives prenatal care — but it does. Your role is to bridge that gap.

Scope of PracticeYou are not an immigration attorney. You do not ask about documentation status unless the client volunteers it. You understand available resources and connect without judgment.

Key Facts

  • Emergency Medicaid covers labor and delivery regardless of immigration status in all states
  • WIC does not ask about immigration status — available to all income-eligible pregnant/postpartum/children
  • FQHCs (Federally Qualified Health Centers) provide care regardless of ability to pay or status
  • Public charge rule: Using emergency Medicaid, WIC, SNAP, CHIP generally does NOT affect immigration applications
  • Language access: Hospitals receiving federal funds must provide interpreter services — this is federal law (Title VI)
  • Fear drives avoidance: Fear of deportation, public charge, or child separation prevents prenatal care engagement

Your Role

What You Can Do

  • → Never ask about documentation status unless she brings it up
  • → Know your local FQHCs, free clinics, and community health workers
  • → Advocate for interpreter services — family members are NOT adequate medical interpreters
  • → Connect with immigration legal aid if she needs guidance on benefits eligibility
