Vital Signs

Blood pressure, pulse, temperature, respiratory rate. These four numbers tell you more about what is happening inside a body than any symptom report. Learning to take them accurately — and knowing when to worry — is foundational to every module that follows. Every other module in this guide builds on your ability to notice vital sign abnormalities.

Scope of PracticeDoulas may take vital signs if trained and equipped. You do not interpret them clinically or make diagnoses. You record, you recognize abnormal ranges, and you communicate findings to the clinical team or provider.

What You'll Learn

  • Take blood pressure, pulse, temperature, and respiratory rate accurately
  • Know normal ranges for pregnancy and postpartum
  • Recognize vital sign combinations that indicate clinical deterioration

The Four Vital Signs

Blood Pressure

Measured in mmHg (systolic/diastolic). Take on bare skin, arm at heart level, after 5 minutes rest. Proper cuff size critical — too small gives falsely high readings. Most adults need a large adult cuff. (See Module 1 for detailed BP technique.)

  • Normal: <120/80 mmHg
  • Concerning: ≥140/90 — diagnostic for gestational HTN
  • Emergency: ≥160/110 — ACOG: treat within 30 minutes
  • Low (concerning): systolic ≤90 — may indicate hemorrhage or sepsis

Pulse (Heart Rate)

Normal resting: 60–100 bpm. Pregnancy increases baseline by 10–20 bpm. Take for 15 seconds × 4 or full 60 seconds if irregular. Location: radial artery (wrist, thumb side).

  • Tachycardia ≥110: maternal early warning sign — hemorrhage? infection? PE? anxiety?
  • Bradycardia ≤50: also concerning — medication effect? vagal response?
  • Key insight: Heart rate is often the FIRST vital sign to change in hemorrhage — before BP drops

Temperature

Oral is standard. Normal: 97.8–99.1°F (36.5–37.3°C).

  • Postpartum fever: ≥100.4°F (38°C) — report to provider same day
  • Hypothermia: <96.8°F (36°C) — can indicate severe sepsis
  • Breast engorgement may cause brief low-grade temp elevation days 3–5 — but if accompanied by other symptoms, evaluate as infection

Respiratory Rate

Count breaths for 30 seconds × 2. Normal: 12–20 breaths per minute. Count without telling her — awareness changes breathing patterns.

  • Tachypnea ≥24: PE? sepsis? heart failure? anxiety?
  • ≥30: critical — escalate immediately
  • Key insight: Respiratory rate is often the FIRST vital sign to change in ANY deterioration. It changes before BP, before HR. Pay attention.
From the PhysicianHere's the hierarchy of vital signs in terms of clinical value: respiratory rate is the canary in the coal mine — it changes first. Heart rate is second. Blood pressure is LAST — by the time BP drops, you're in serious trouble. Most people (including many clinicians) skip respiratory rate. You count it at every visit. It takes 30 seconds.

Maternal Early Warning Criteria

⚠ Any of These Requires Provider Notification

  • Heart rate ≥110 or ≤50
  • Respiratory rate ≥30 or ≤10
  • BP systolic ≥160 or ≤90
  • BP diastolic ≥100
  • Oxygen saturation <95% (if you have a pulse oximeter)
  • Altered mental status — confusion, unusual drowsiness, agitation
  • Oliguria — not urinating for 4+ hours (ask about it)

Pattern recognition matters more than single values. Tachycardia + tachypnea together is more concerning than either alone. Fever + tachycardia = infection until proven otherwise. Tachycardia + cool extremities = hemorrhage until proven otherwise.

Your Doula Vital Signs Kit

  • Automatic blood pressure monitor — validated for pregnancy (Omron recommended). With large adult cuff.
  • Digital thermometer — oral
  • Watch with second hand (or phone timer) — for pulse and respiratory rate
  • Pulse oximeter (optional but recommended) — <$20, clips on finger
  • Notebook or phone — for recording values with time stamps

Test Your Understanding

1. What is typically the FIRST vital sign to change when a patient is deteriorating?

Respiratory rate is the earliest indicator of deterioration — it changes before HR and long before BP. Yet it's the most frequently skipped vital sign. Count it every time.

2. Postpartum fever is defined as:

≥100.4°F (38°C) = postpartum fever. Report to provider same day. Low-grade temp with engorgement (days 3–5) may be benign, but evaluate in context.

3. Tachycardia + cool, clammy extremities most likely indicates:

Tachycardia + cool extremities = the body is compensating for blood loss by constricting peripheral blood vessels. This is compensated shock (Module 2) — act before the crash.

4. The most common error in blood pressure measurement is:

A too-small cuff artificially elevates readings. Most adults need a large adult cuff. This single error has led to unnecessary interventions and missed diagnoses.

5. Fever + tachycardia in a postpartum client most likely indicates:

Fever + tachycardia = infection until proven otherwise. Add the sepsis screening triad (Module 3): temperature + heart rate + appearance. Two of three flagged = call provider now.
