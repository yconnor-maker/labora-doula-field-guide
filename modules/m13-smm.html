Severe Maternal Morbidity

For every maternal death, there are 100 women who nearly died. Severe maternal morbidity (SMM) is the "near-miss" — the ICU admission, the emergency hysterectomy, the blood transfusion that saved her life. Understanding SMM helps you recognize deterioration before it becomes death.

Scope of PracticeYou do not assess clinical severity. You notice patterns — the subtle changes that precede emergencies. You communicate what you observe. The clinical team makes the diagnosis.

What You'll Learn

  • Define severe maternal morbidity and the CDC's 21 indicators
  • Understand the scale of SMM in the United States
  • Recognize the racial disparities in near-miss outcomes
  • Identify early warning signs of clinical deterioration
  • Understand what ICU admission means for the postpartum person
  • Support families through a near-miss experience

What Is Severe Maternal Morbidity?

The CDC defines SMM as unexpected outcomes of labor and delivery that result in significant short- or long-term consequences to a woman's health. The CDC tracks 21 indicators including: blood transfusion (≥4 units), hysterectomy, ICU admission, mechanical ventilation, eclampsia, sepsis, renal failure, DIC, cardiac arrest, and shock.

In the US, SMM affects approximately 50,000–60,000 women per year — and the rate has been increasing. Blood transfusion accounts for the majority of SMM events.

From the PhysicianThe near-miss concept reframes maternal safety. Instead of only counting the women who died, we study the women who almost died — because the same system failures are present, the same red flags were missed, the same delays occurred. The difference between death and near-miss is often luck, not superior care.

Racial Disparities in SMM

Black women experience SMM at 2–3× the rate of white women. This disparity persists after controlling for income, education, insurance status, and comorbidities. The causes are structural:

  • Hospitals serving predominantly Black communities have higher complication rates regardless of patient risk
  • Black women's pain and symptoms are more likely to be dismissed or minimized
  • Weathering — the cumulative physiologic toll of chronic racism — increases baseline cardiovascular and metabolic risk
  • Delayed recognition: the same symptoms may be evaluated faster in white patients

As a doula, you are often the person who amplifies her voice when the system is not listening. This is not activism — it is clinical advocacy that saves lives.

Early Warning Signs of Deterioration

⚠ Maternal Early Warning Signs

  • Heart rate ≥110 or ≤50 beats per minute
  • Respiratory rate ≥30 or ≤10 breaths per minute
  • Blood pressure: systolic ≥160 or ≤90, diastolic ≥100
  • Oxygen saturation <95%
  • Altered mental status — confusion, agitation, unresponsiveness
  • Oliguria — urine output <35 mL/hour over 2 hours
  • Non-remitting headache or shortness of breath
  • Disproportionate pain — "something is wrong"

Your Role

  • → Trust what you observe. If something looks wrong, say something.
  • → Use specific language: "Her breathing seems faster than it was an hour ago" is more useful than "she seems off"
  • → Advocate for response: if the nurse dismisses a concern and your client continues to decline, escalate — ask for the charge nurse or the attending physician
  • → After the event: help her process. A near-miss is traumatic even when the outcome is "good."

Supporting Families After a Near-Miss

Surviving a near-miss is not the same as being okay. Many women who experience SMM develop PTSD, depression, fear of future pregnancy, relationship strain, and prolonged physical recovery. The emotional aftermath is often underrecognized.

  • Validate: "What happened to you was serious. Your feelings about it are valid."
  • Connect to mental health support — birth trauma therapy, PTSD-informed care
  • Help her understand what happened — many women leave the hospital confused about their own medical events
  • Support her in requesting medical records and debriefing with her provider

Test Your Understanding

1. How many women experience severe maternal morbidity in the US each year?

Approximately 50,000–60,000 women per year experience SMM — for every maternal death, there are roughly 100 near-misses.

2. Black women experience SMM at what rate compared to white women?

The 2–3× disparity persists after controlling for income, education, and insurance — indicating structural, not individual, causes.

3. Which of the following is a maternal early warning sign?

Tachycardia ≥110 is a maternal early warning sign that should trigger clinical assessment. Other signs: respiratory rate ≥30, BP ≥160/100, SpO2 <95%, altered mental status.

4. The most common SMM indicator is:

Blood transfusion (≥4 units) is the most common CDC-tracked SMM indicator, reflecting the prevalence of hemorrhage-related morbidity.

5. After a near-miss, the doula should:

Surviving is not the same as being okay. Validate, connect to birth trauma therapy, and help her request records and debrief with her provider.
