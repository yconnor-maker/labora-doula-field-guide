Preterm Labor

When labor starts before 37 weeks, every hour matters. The doula who understands tocolysis, corticosteroid timing, and NICU realities becomes a critical bridge between a terrified family and a clinical team moving at emergency speed.

Scope of PracticeYou do not assess cervical change or diagnose preterm labor. You recognize contraction patterns, help your client communicate with her provider, and prepare the family emotionally for what may come.

What You'll Learn

  • Distinguish Braxton-Hicks from true preterm contractions
  • Understand the clinical significance of gestational age thresholds
  • Know what tocolytics, corticosteroids, and magnesium for neuroprotection do
  • Prepare families for NICU admission
  • Support a client through the emotional crisis of preterm birth

What Counts as Preterm?

Preterm birth is delivery before 37 weeks of gestation. It is the leading cause of neonatal morbidity and mortality in the United States, affecting approximately 10% of all pregnancies. But not all preterm births carry the same risk:

  • Late preterm (34–36 weeks): Generally good outcomes, but still at risk for feeding difficulties, jaundice, temperature instability
  • Moderate preterm (32–33 weeks): Usually requires NICU stay; respiratory support often needed
  • Very preterm (28–31 weeks): Significant NICU stay; higher risk of complications
  • Extremely preterm (<28 weeks): Intensive care required; survival rates improving but long-term complications common
From the PhysicianThe most important clinical window is 23–34 weeks. Before 23 weeks, viability is limited and decisions are agonizing. Between 24–34 weeks, every day we can delay delivery improves outcomes — especially if we can get corticosteroids on board. After 34 weeks, the risk-benefit of tocolysis shifts. Your client needs to understand: we are buying time, not preventing birth.

Braxton-Hicks vs. Preterm Labor

Many pregnant people experience Braxton-Hicks contractions — irregular, painless tightenings that do not change the cervix. The challenge is distinguishing these from true preterm labor.

⚠ Signs That Suggest True Preterm Labor

  • Regular contractions — every 10 minutes or more frequently
  • Contractions that increase in intensity and don't stop with rest or hydration
  • Menstrual-like cramping, low back pain that comes and goes
  • Pelvic pressure — "the baby feels really low"
  • Vaginal discharge change — watery, mucousy, or bloody (bloody show)
  • Leaking fluid — possible preterm rupture of membranes

What You Can Do

  • → Help her time contractions: frequency, duration, and whether they're getting closer
  • → Encourage hydration and position change — if contractions persist, call the provider
  • → If contractions are regular and before 37 weeks: "Let's call your provider now."
  • → If fluid leaking: go to L&D immediately. Do not wait. PPROM requires evaluation.

What the Clinical Team Does

Tocolytics — Buying Time

Tocolytic medications slow or stop contractions. They do not prevent preterm birth — they buy 48 hours for corticosteroids to work. Common tocolytics include nifedipine (calcium channel blocker), indomethacin (NSAID, used <32 weeks), and magnesium sulfate (also used for neuroprotection).

Corticosteroids — The 48-Hour Window

Betamethasone or dexamethasone given to the mother accelerates fetal lung maturation. The full benefit requires 48 hours. This is why delaying delivery — even by two days — can be life-changing for the baby. Recommended between 24–34 weeks gestation (with consideration for late preterm 34–36 weeks in some cases).

Magnesium Sulfate for Neuroprotection

When delivery before 32 weeks is anticipated, magnesium sulfate is given to reduce the risk of cerebral palsy in the neonate. This is separate from its use in preeclampsia — here it protects the baby's brain.

Antibiotics for GBS and PPROM

If membranes have ruptured, antibiotics are given to prevent infection and prolong latency. If GBS status is unknown or positive, prophylactic antibiotics are given during labor.

Preparing for the NICU

If preterm delivery is likely, the family needs preparation — not just logistically, but emotionally.

What You Can Do

  • → Offer to arrange a NICU tour if time allows
  • → Explain that NICU babies may not cry at birth — this is expected, not a crisis
  • → Help her understand skin-to-skin (kangaroo care) may be delayed but is still the goal
  • → Connect with lactation: pumping may start before baby can nurse
  • → Validate grief: the birth she planned is not the birth she'll have. This loss is real.
  • → Help with logistics: who watches other children, who brings supplies, who communicates with family

Case Scenario

Your client is 31 weeks pregnant with her second baby. She calls you at 10 PM saying she's been having "cramps" every 8 minutes for the last hour. She drank water and laid on her left side but they haven't stopped. She says it feels "different from last time — more like period cramps, not Braxton-Hicks." She also noticed some mucousy discharge.

What do you tell her?

ResponseRegular contractions every 8 minutes at 31 weeks, not resolving with hydration and rest, with mucous discharge = call the provider and go to L&D now. At 31 weeks, the clinical team needs to evaluate cervical change, consider tocolysis, and ideally administer corticosteroids if delivery is imminent. This is exactly the clinical window where buying 48 hours changes neonatal outcomes. Do not tell her to "wait and see."

Test Your Understanding

1. Why are corticosteroids given in threatened preterm labor?

Betamethasone/dexamethasone accelerate fetal lung maturation. The full benefit requires 48 hours — which is why tocolysis is used to buy time.

2. At what gestational age is magnesium sulfate given for fetal neuroprotection?

Magnesium for neuroprotection is given when delivery before 32 weeks is anticipated. It reduces the risk of cerebral palsy in premature neonates.

3. Your client at 33 weeks reports fluid leaking from her vagina. You should:

Possible PPROM (preterm premature rupture of membranes) requires immediate evaluation. No bathing, no waiting. L&D triage now.

4. The primary purpose of tocolytic medications is to:

Tocolytics do not prevent preterm birth — they buy time. The 48-hour window for corticosteroid benefit is the clinical goal.

5. How can a doula best prepare a family facing likely NICU admission?

Validate the loss of the planned birth, explain NICU basics (baby may not cry, skin-to-skin may be delayed, pumping may start early), and help with practical logistics.
