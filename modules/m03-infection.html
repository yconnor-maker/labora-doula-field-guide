Postpartum Infection

Infection is the third leading cause of maternal death. Endometritis, wound infections, mastitis, UTI, and sepsis can all present subtly and escalate rapidly. A low-grade fever on day 3 can become a septic ICU admission by day 5. You are looking at incisions, checking temperatures, and asking about lochia smell at every postpartum visit — and that vigilance saves lives.

Scope of PracticeYou do not diagnose infection. You recognize fever, wound changes, foul-smelling lochia, and systemic symptoms — and escalate promptly. A thermometer should be in every doula bag.

What You'll Learn

  • Recognize signs of endometritis, wound infection, mastitis, and UTI
  • Understand what fever means in the postpartum period
  • Know the early warning signs of sepsis
  • Observe wound healing without performing clinical assessment
  • Use the sepsis screening triad: temperature + heart rate + appearance

Common Postpartum Infections

Endometritis

Infection of the uterine lining — the most common post-cesarean infection. Occurs in 1–3% of vaginal deliveries and 5–10% of cesarean deliveries. Signs: fever (often the first clue), uterine tenderness (she winces when you press on her belly or when the uterus contracts), foul-smelling lochia, tachycardia. Risk factors: prolonged labor, prolonged rupture of membranes (>18 hours), cesarean (especially after labor), multiple cervical exams, retained products.

Wound Infection

Cesarean incision or perineal laceration site. Signs: redness spreading beyond the incision edges (mark the border with a pen to track), warmth, swelling, purulent drainage, wound opening (dehiscence). Onset typically 4–7 days postpartum. Ask her to show you the incision at every visit. Most surgical site infections become apparent after discharge.

Mastitis

Breast tissue infection, usually unilateral. Signs: red, warm, firm wedge-shaped area on one breast, fever ≥101°F (38.3°C), flu-like symptoms (body aches, chills, fatigue). Often confused with engorgement initially. Distinguishing feature: engorgement is bilateral and without fever; mastitis is unilateral with fever. Continue breastfeeding — the milk is safe and emptying the breast is therapeutic.

UTI / Pyelonephritis

Urinary tract infection can progress to kidney infection (pyelonephritis). Signs: burning with urination, frequency, urgency, flank pain, fever. Catheterization during labor increases risk. Pyelonephritis presents with high fever, shaking chills, flank pain — this is a hospital admission.

From the PhysicianThe thing about postpartum infection that kills people: the early signs are subtle and get explained away. "She's just tired." "New moms are always warm." "Her incision is a little red, that's normal." Trust what you see. If her incision was pink yesterday and today there's a spreading area of redness, if her lochia suddenly smells different, if she has a fever with a racing heart — these are not variations of normal. These are infection until proven otherwise.

Sepsis — When Infection Goes Systemic

Sepsis is the body's overwhelming, life-threatening response to infection. It causes organ failure and death if not treated rapidly. Maternal sepsis is the third leading cause of maternal death. It can progress from "she looks a little off" to multi-organ failure in hours.

⚠ Sepsis Warning Signs — This Is An Emergency

  • Temperature ≥100.4°F (38°C) OR <96.8°F (36°C) — hypothermia can indicate SEVERE sepsis
  • Heart rate ≥110 at rest
  • Respiratory rate ≥22
  • Confusion, difficulty staying awake, not making sense
  • Skin mottling — blotchy, discolored patches
  • Not making urine
  • Shaking chills (rigors)
  • "She looks sick" — trust your instinct. You know what "normal tired" looks like in your clients. Sepsis looks different.

The Sepsis Screening Triad

At every postpartum visit, assess three things:

1
Temperature

Take it. Any fever ≥100.4°F or hypothermia <96.8°F → flag.

2
Heart rate

Feel her pulse for 15 seconds, multiply by 4. Resting HR ≥110 → flag.

3
Appearance

Does she look sick? Skin color, alertness, coherence. "She just doesn't look right" → flag.

If 2 of 3 are flagged: call provider immediately. If all 3: call 911.

Case Scenario

You visit your client on day 6 after cesarean delivery. She says she's "just really tired" and "feels like she's getting the flu." Her temperature is 101.2°F. Her pulse is 118. You ask to see her incision — the right side has new redness spreading about 2 inches beyond the incision edge, and there's a small amount of yellowish drainage. Her lochia smells stronger than it did at your last visit.

ResponseFever + tachycardia + spreading wound redness + purulent drainage + malodorous lochia at 6 days post-cesarean = possible wound infection AND endometritis. 2 of the 3 sepsis screening triad items are flagged (temp + HR). Call the provider now: "Day 6 post-cesarean. Temp 101.2, HR 118. Expanding incisional erythema with purulent drainage. Malodorous lochia. Requesting same-day evaluation." Do not minimize. Do not wait until tomorrow.

Test Your Understanding

1. Which postpartum infection is most common after cesarean delivery?

Endometritis occurs in 5–10% of cesarean deliveries — the most common post-operative infectious complication. Signs: fever, uterine tenderness, foul-smelling lochia.

2. In sepsis, body temperature may be:

Hypothermia (<96.8°F/36°C) can indicate severe sepsis — the body is losing its ability to mount a fever response. This is more dangerous than a high fever.

3. How do you distinguish mastitis from engorgement?

Unilateral red, warm, firm wedge + fever + flu-like symptoms = mastitis. Bilateral fullness without fever = engorgement. Mastitis requires antibiotics if not improving in 12–24 hours.

4. The sepsis screening triad assesses:

Temperature (fever or hypothermia) + heart rate (≥110) + appearance (does she look sick?) = the three assessments a doula can do at every visit that screen for sepsis.

5. A useful trick for tracking wound infection progression is:

Drawing a line at the border of redness with a pen lets you objectively track whether the infection is spreading. If redness extends beyond the line at your next check — it's progressing and needs medical evaluation.
