Advocacy & Discrimination

Medical racism is not a perception — it is a documented phenomenon with measurable outcomes. Black women are 3–4× more likely to die from pregnancy-related causes. Their pain is undertreated, their symptoms dismissed, their autonomy undermined. The doula is a witness, an amplifier, and a buffer between a system that doesn't always listen and a woman who deserves to be heard.

Scope of PracticeYou do not provide legal advice or physically intervene in clinical care. You support informed consent, witness interactions, document what you observe, help clients access complaint mechanisms, and amplify her voice when the system doesn't hear it.

What You'll Learn

  • Understand informed consent as an ongoing process, not a form
  • Know the right to refuse any treatment at any time
  • Recognize common patterns of medical discrimination
  • Use specific language to advocate without confrontation
  • Document interactions that may constitute discrimination
  • Know the complaint pathways: hospital, state, and federal

Informed Consent Is a Right

Every person has the right to:

  • Receive information about proposed interventions: benefits, risks, alternatives, and the option of doing nothing
  • Ask questions — as many as needed
  • Accept or refuse any treatment — even if the provider disagrees
  • Change her mind at any time — consent given earlier can be withdrawn
  • Make decisions free from coercion, threats, or pressure

Informed consent is not a form. It is an ongoing conversation. A signature under pressure is not consent. "We're doing this now" without explanation is not consent.

From the PhysicianI've watched colleagues tell women "if you don't agree to this cesarean, your baby will die." That is coercion, not consent. Real informed consent sounds like: "Here is what I'm seeing on the monitor. I'm recommending cesarean because [specific reason]. The alternative is [alternative]. The risk of waiting is [specific risk]. I want you to understand this so you can make a decision." When I hear a provider use fear as the primary motivator — that's a red flag.

Recognizing Discrimination

Medical discrimination is often subtle. Patterns to notice:

  • Pain dismissal: "You're fine" when she's clearly not. Studies show Black patients receive less pain medication for the same conditions.
  • Tone shift: The provider speaks differently to her than to other patients — condescending, impatient, dismissive.
  • Autonomy override: Decisions made without her input. Procedures begun without explanation. "We're going to do X now."
  • Assumptions: Asking about drug use, insurance status, or baby's father based on race or appearance.
  • Delayed response: Call light takes longer. Questions are brushed off. She waits longer for assessment.

Advocacy Language

Your power is in words, presence, and documentation — not confrontation. Use these phrases:

Advocacy Scripts

  • → "She has some questions before she decides. Can you explain that again?"
  • → "She said she's in pain. Can we address that?"
  • → "Can you walk us through the benefits, risks, and alternatives?"
  • → "She'd like a few minutes to discuss this with her partner before deciding."
  • → "Is there a medical reason this needs to happen right now, or can we take some time?"
  • → If dismissed: "I want to make sure this is documented. Can you note in the chart that she reported [symptom] and the response was [response]?"

The documentation request is powerful. When you ask a provider to document that they declined to address a concern, behavior often changes immediately.

Complaint Pathways

  • Hospital patient advocate/ombudsman: First step. Every hospital has one. File during the stay if possible.
  • Hospital administration: Written complaint to the chief medical officer or chief nursing officer.
  • State medical board: For physician misconduct. File at your state's medical board website.
  • State nursing board: For nursing misconduct.
  • OCR/HHS: Office for Civil Rights, Department of Health and Human Services. For discrimination complaints based on race, color, national origin, sex, disability, or age. File at hhs.gov/ocr.
  • Joint Commission: Hospital accreditation body. Complaints at jointcommission.org.

Documentation for the Doula

If you witness concerning interactions, document contemporaneously:

  • Date, time, location
  • Who was present (names, titles)
  • What was said — direct quotes when possible
  • What was done (or not done)
  • Your client's response
  • Keep this in a secure location — it may be needed later

Test Your Understanding

1. Informed consent requires:

Informed consent is a conversation, not a form. It requires understanding of benefits, risks, alternatives, and the right to refuse — free from coercion.

2. A powerful advocacy tool when a provider dismisses your client's concern is:

"Can you document in the chart that she reported [symptom] and the response was [response]?" This request creates accountability and often changes behavior immediately.

3. For a discrimination complaint based on race, the federal pathway is:

OCR at HHS handles discrimination complaints under Title VI of the Civil Rights Act for facilities receiving federal funding (which includes all hospitals accepting Medicare/Medicaid).

4. "We're going to break your water now" without explanation or asking for consent is:

Amniotomy without explanation, discussion of risks/benefits, and explicit consent is a violation. Even in emergencies, brief explanation is possible and ethically required.

5. The doula should NEVER:

Your power is in words, presence, and documentation. Never physically intervene in clinical care — this puts you, your client, and your profession at risk. Verbal advocacy is your tool.
