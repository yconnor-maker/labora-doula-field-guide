The System Wasn't Built for Her

One woman. One pregnancy. Every system failure. This is the story of Janelle — and every client whose survival depends not on medicine, but on connection.

Week 12

The Positive Test and the Insurance Gap

Janelle is 24. She works two part-time jobs — neither offers health insurance. She's been uninsured for 8 months since aging off her mother's plan. The positive pregnancy test came from a dollar store kit in a gas station bathroom.

She calls her old doctor's office. "Do you have insurance?" No. "We can't see you without insurance. Call the Medicaid office." She calls Medicaid. Hold time: 47 minutes. She hangs up. She has to clock in at her second job in 20 minutes.

She doesn't have a computer. She doesn't know healthcare.gov exists. She doesn't know that pregnancy is a qualifying life event for marketplace enrollment. She doesn't know about presumptive Medicaid eligibility. She doesn't know about FQHCs.

She knows she's pregnant. She doesn't know how to see a doctor.

System Failure

42% of US births are covered by Medicaid — but enrollment barriers prevent timely access. Hold times, paperwork complexity, and lack of in-person navigators mean that women like Janelle start prenatal care late or not at all. Late prenatal care is independently associated with preterm birth, preeclampsia, and maternal mortality.

Week 18

The First Appointment — Six Weeks Late

Her cousin told her about the community health center. An FQHC. They see everyone regardless of ability to pay. She finally has her first prenatal appointment at 18 weeks — 6 weeks later than recommended.

Her blood pressure is 138/88. Not yet diagnostic for gestational hypertension, but trending. No one told her she has risk factors. No one asked where she lives, what she eats, whether she feels safe at home.

She fills out paperwork. Checks "no" for domestic violence because her boyfriend is sitting in the waiting room reading the form over her shoulder.

System Failure

The IPV screening happened in the waiting room. With her partner present. The form was in English — her first language, but a written form doesn't create the safety to disclose. She needed to be screened alone, verbally, by someone she trusts. She checked "no" because "yes" would have meant going home to a beating.

"The social determinants of health don't just affect health. They ARE health. Address them or watch women die."

Week 28

The Eviction Notice and the Rising Pressure

Her boyfriend left. The rent was in his name. She gets a 30-day eviction notice while 7 months pregnant. She calls 211. The shelters are full. Her mother's apartment is a 1-bedroom with 4 people already in it.

She sleeps on the couch. She misses her next two prenatal appointments because she's trying to find housing. Her blood pressure, unchecked, climbs to 152/96. She has a headache she attributes to stress. Her ankles are swollen. She assumes it's pregnancy.

No one is watching.

Week 32

The Doula Changes the Trajectory

A community health worker at the FQHC refers her to a doula program — Medicaid-covered in her state. She meets you.

First visit: you take her blood pressure. 156/98. You ask about headaches. Yes, daily. Visual changes? "Sometimes sparkly." Swelling? "My face looks puffy."

You don't diagnose preeclampsia. You call the FQHC. You drive her to triage. They diagnose severe-range preeclampsia. She's admitted. Betamethasone for fetal lung maturity. Magnesium started. She delivers at 34 weeks. Baby goes to NICU for 2 weeks. Both survive.

This Is the Connector Tier

Janelle's story is not unusual. It is the American maternal health experience for millions of women who are uninsured, underinsured, housing-unstable, food-insecure, fleeing violence, navigating immigration barriers, or incarcerated.

The clinical modules teach you to catch the hemorrhage, the preeclampsia, the cardiomyopathy. The Connector modules teach you to catch everything else that's killing her — the systems designed to confuse, exclude, and fail her.

You learned insurance navigation. Housing resources. IPV screening. Immigration barriers. Advocacy language. Complaint pathways. Every one of those skills showed up in Janelle's story. Every one of them was the difference between her survival and a maternal death that would have been classified as "not preventable."

It was preventable. You prevented it.
