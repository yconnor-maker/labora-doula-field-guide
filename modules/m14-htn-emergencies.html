Hypertensive Emergencies

Eclampsia. HELLP syndrome. Posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome. These are the conditions that kill women who "looked fine" an hour ago. Module 1 taught you the basics — this module teaches you the endgame.

Scope of PracticeYou do not treat hypertensive emergencies. You do not interpret blood pressure values. You recognize the symptoms that precede crisis — the headache that won't quit, the vision changes, the confusion — and you escalate before the seizure.

What You'll Learn

  • Understand the progression from preeclampsia to eclampsia
  • Recognize HELLP syndrome as a distinct, life-threatening entity
  • Know the neurologic warning signs that precede seizure or stroke
  • Understand what magnesium sulfate does and why it matters
  • Know the doula's role during an eclamptic seizure
  • Navigate the postpartum window where these conditions peak

The Spectrum of Hypertensive Disease

Preeclampsia is not a single event — it's a spectrum. It can progress from mild elevations in blood pressure to catastrophic organ failure. Understanding this spectrum is critical because the transition from stable to unstable can happen in hours.

Preeclampsia with Severe Features

Defined by blood pressure ≥160/110 mmHg on two occasions at least 4 hours apart, OR severe features including: thrombocytopenia (platelets <100,000), liver enzyme elevation (twice upper limit of normal), renal insufficiency (creatinine >1.1), pulmonary edema, or new-onset cerebral or visual disturbances.

Eclampsia

A generalized tonic-clonic seizure in a person with preeclampsia. This is what happens when the disease reaches the brain. Eclampsia can occur before, during, or after delivery — and approximately 40% of eclamptic seizures occur postpartum, often within the first 48 hours but sometimes up to 6 weeks after delivery.

HELLP Syndrome

Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets. A variant of severe preeclampsia that specifically attacks the liver and blood system. Women with HELLP often present with right upper quadrant pain, nausea/vomiting (often mistaken for GI illness), and can deteriorate to hepatic rupture or DIC.

From the PhysicianThe most dangerous thing about preeclampsia is that it can masquerade as normal pregnancy discomfort. "My head hurts" can be a headache — or it can be cerebral edema. "I feel swollen" can be normal — or it can be anasarca with impending organ failure. The red flags are: headache that doesn't respond to acetaminophen, visual changes (flashing lights, blurriness, blind spots), right upper quadrant pain, and confusion. These are not normal pregnancy symptoms.

Neurologic Warning Signs

The brain is the most dangerous target of hypertensive disease. When blood pressure overwhelms the brain's ability to regulate its own blood flow (cerebral autoregulation), edema develops — this is called posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome (PRES). If untreated, PRES can progress to hemorrhagic stroke.

⚠ Neurologic Red Flags — Escalate Immediately

  • Severe headache unresponsive to acetaminophen or rest
  • Visual changes: blurriness, flashing lights, blind spots, double vision
  • Confusion, difficulty speaking, or altered consciousness
  • New weakness or numbness on one side
  • Seizure (generalized or focal)
  • Epigastric or right upper quadrant pain (liver involvement)
  • Sudden severe swelling of face and hands (not just ankles)

What the Clinical Team Does

Seizure Protocol (Eclampsia)

1
Protect the airway

Turn the patient on her side (left lateral). Do NOT place anything in her mouth. Suction if needed.

2
Magnesium sulfate

Loading dose: 4–6g IV over 15–20 minutes. Maintenance: 1–2g/hour continuous infusion. This is the gold standard for eclampsia seizure prevention and treatment.

3
Blood pressure control

IV labetalol, IV hydralazine, or oral nifedipine. Goal: reduce severe-range BP within 30–60 minutes. ACOG recommends treatment within 30 minutes of confirmed severe-range BP.

4
Fetal monitoring

Continuous fetal monitoring during stabilization. Fetal distress may require delivery.

5
Plan for delivery

Delivery is the definitive treatment for preeclampsia. Timing depends on severity, gestational age, and maternal/fetal status.

Magnesium Sulfate — Why It Matters

Magnesium sulfate reduces the risk of eclamptic seizure by approximately 50%. It works by stabilizing neuronal membranes, reducing cerebral vasospasm, and improving cerebral blood flow. Your client may feel flushed, warm, or sedated — these are expected effects. The team monitors reflexes, respiratory rate, and urine output to prevent toxicity.

Magnesium Toxicity SignsLoss of deep tendon reflexes → respiratory depression → cardiac arrest. The team checks reflexes regularly. If your client's knee-jerk reflex disappears or she becomes difficult to arouse, alert the nurse immediately — these can be early signs of magnesium toxicity.

The Postpartum Window

This is the part that catches everyone off guard: preeclampsia and eclampsia can develop AFTER delivery. The postpartum period — especially the first 48 hours, but extending up to 6 weeks — is a high-risk window. Many women are discharged before symptoms appear.

At home visits, you are sometimes the only trained person watching for these signs. If your postpartum client reports persistent severe headache, visual changes, or confusion — this is an emergency, not a follow-up visit concern.

Your Role During a Hypertensive Emergency

What You Can Do

  • → Know the symptoms. Teach them to your client prenatally and postpartum.
  • → If she seizes: do NOT restrain her. Clear the area. Turn her on her side. Call for help.
  • → After the seizure: she may be confused, combative, or unresponsive. This is expected postictal state.
  • → At home: call 911. Do not drive to the hospital. Seizure = ambulance.
  • → Support the partner. They've just watched something terrifying. You are the calm in the room.
  • → Postpartum visits: ask about headache, vision, and swelling at EVERY visit. Don't assume discharge = safety.

Case Scenario

You're visiting your client at home, 4 days postpartum. She had a "normal" delivery with no complications. She tells you she has a terrible headache that won't go away — "worse than any headache I've ever had." She took Tylenol twice and it didn't help. She also mentions her vision has been "sparkly" since this morning. She looks a bit puffy in the face.

What do you do?

ResponseThis is postpartum preeclampsia with severe features until proven otherwise. Severe headache unresponsive to acetaminophen + visual changes + facial edema at day 4 postpartum = call the provider NOW. Do not wait. Do not tell her to "monitor it." If you cannot reach the provider quickly, take her to L&D triage directly (not the ER — L&D). If she becomes confused or seizes, call 911. Document: "Day 4 PP, severe HA unresponsive to acetaminophen ×2, visual 'sparkles' since AM, facial edema."

Test Your Understanding

1. What percentage of eclamptic seizures occur postpartum?

Approximately 40% of eclamptic seizures occur postpartum. The postpartum period is a high-risk window that extends up to 6 weeks.

2. HELLP syndrome involves:

HELLP = Hemolysis + Elevated Liver enzymes + Low Platelets. It often presents with RUQ pain and nausea — frequently misdiagnosed as GI illness.

3. Your client on a mag drip tells you she feels "really warm and flushed." What's your response?

Flushing, warmth, and mild sedation are EXPECTED side effects of magnesium sulfate. Toxicity signs are: loss of reflexes, respiratory depression, and severe lethargy. The team monitors reflexes regularly.

4. If your postpartum client at home has a seizure, you should:

NEVER put anything in the mouth. NEVER restrain. Turn on side (recovery position), clear area of hazards, call 911. Ambulance — do not drive. Seizure in postpartum = eclampsia until proven otherwise.

5. ACOG recommends treating severe-range blood pressure within:

ACOG's committee opinion is clear: severe-range BP (≥160/110) should be treated within 30 minutes. Delayed treatment is associated with maternal stroke and death.
