Hypertension in Pregnancy

Hypertensive disorders of pregnancy are the second leading cause of maternal death in the United States. This module teaches you to recognize the warning signs, understand what the numbers mean, and know when a headache is not just a headache.

Scope of PracticeYou may take blood pressure if trained and equipped, but you do not interpret clinical values or make diagnoses. You recognize warning symptoms — headache, visual changes, epigastric pain — and escalate immediately.

What You'll Learn

  • Classify the hypertensive disorders of pregnancy
  • Know what blood pressure numbers mean and when they're dangerous
  • Recognize preeclampsia warning symptoms
  • Understand why postpartum hypertension catches everyone off guard
  • Use an escalation script for concerning symptoms

Types of Hypertensive Disorders

  • Chronic hypertension: Pre-existing HTN diagnosed before 20 weeks or before pregnancy
  • Gestational hypertension: New HTN after 20 weeks without proteinuria or severe features
  • Preeclampsia: HTN after 20 weeks + proteinuria OR severe features (organ damage)
  • Preeclampsia with severe features: BP ≥160/110, organ involvement, neurologic symptoms
  • Chronic HTN with superimposed preeclampsia: Worsening of pre-existing HTN with new features

Understanding the Numbers

  • Normal: <120/80 mmHg
  • Elevated: 120–129/<80
  • Stage 1 HTN: 130–139/80–89
  • Stage 2 HTN: ≥140/90
  • Severe range: ≥160/110 — ACOG: treat within 30 minutes

⚠ Urgent Warning Signs

  • Blood pressure ≥160/110 on any reading
  • Severe headache not relieved by acetaminophen
  • Visual changes — blurriness, spots, flashing lights
  • Right upper quadrant or epigastric pain
  • Sudden swelling of face and hands
  • Confusion or altered mental status
  • Shortness of breath or chest tightness

Your Role

1
Know the symptoms

Teach them to your client prenatally. Review at every visit.

2
Ask at every visit

"Any headaches? Vision changes? Upper belly pain? Swelling?"

3
If yes: call provider

"She is ___ weeks pregnant/postpartum with [symptoms]. She needs evaluation today."

4
Severe symptoms: go to L&D

Don't wait for a callback. Go directly to L&D triage, not the ER.

Test Your Understanding

1. Preeclampsia is defined as hypertension after ___ weeks with proteinuria or severe features:

Preeclampsia develops after 20 weeks. Hypertension before 20 weeks is classified as chronic hypertension.

2. ACOG recommends treating severe-range BP (≥160/110) within:

Delayed treatment of severe-range BP is associated with maternal stroke and death. 30 minutes is the ACOG standard.

3. Your postpartum client has a headache and her BP at home reads 165/105. You should:

Severe-range BP + headache = emergency. Go to L&D triage directly — do not wait.
