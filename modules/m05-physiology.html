Postpartum Physiology

The postpartum body undergoes more rapid physiologic change than at any other time in adult life. Understanding what's normal — uterine involution, lochia progression, hormonal shifts, cardiovascular changes — helps you distinguish expected recovery from dangerous deviation.

Scope of PracticeYou do not assess clinical recovery. You understand the normal trajectory so you can notice when something deviates from it.

Uterine Involution

The uterus shrinks from the size of a watermelon to a pear in 6 weeks. Immediately after delivery, the fundus is palpable at the umbilicus. It descends approximately one fingerbreadth per day. By 2 weeks, it should be back in the pelvis and no longer palpable abdominally. A uterus that is not involuting (staying large, boggy, tender) may indicate retained products or infection.

Hormonal Shifts

Estrogen and progesterone drop precipitously after placental delivery — the most dramatic hormonal shift in human physiology. This drives the "baby blues" (mood lability days 2–14), milk production (prolactin rises as estrogen falls), night sweats, hair loss (telogen effluvium at 3–6 months), and libido changes. These are all normal — but duration and severity matter.

Cardiovascular Changes

Blood volume increases 40–50% during pregnancy. After delivery, this extra volume is redistributed and excreted. Expect: postpartum diuresis (increased urination for 2–5 days), mild tachycardia, and the potential for fluid overload in vulnerable hearts (cardiomyopathy, Module 16).

⚠ Deviations From Normal

  • Uterus not shrinking — still palpable above pubic bone at 2+ weeks
  • Lochia that becomes foul-smelling
  • Night sweats with fever (infection, not just hormones)
  • Mood symptoms persisting beyond 2 weeks (PMADs, Module 11)
  • Inability to urinate or urinary retention postpartum
