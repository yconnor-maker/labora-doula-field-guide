Justice-Involved Clients

Approximately 55,000 pregnant people are incarcerated in the US each year. Shackling during labor still occurs despite bans in most states. Prenatal care in jails and prisons is often inadequate. Reentry planning determines whether she keeps her baby. Doulas working in jails and prisons are providing some of the most critical birth support in America.

Scope of PracticeYou follow facility rules. You do not interfere with custody proceedings. You provide emotional support, birth advocacy, and reentry resource connection.

Key Issues

  • Shackling: Federal and most state laws prohibit restraints during labor. Know your state's law. Document violations.
  • Prenatal care: Often delayed, inconsistent, or inadequate. Advocate for timely appointments and medication continuity.
  • Separation at birth: In most facilities, baby is removed within 24–72 hours. Some states have prison nursery programs.
  • MAT access: Pregnant incarcerated people with OUD often have MAT discontinued — this is medically dangerous (Module 21).
  • Reentry: Housing, Medicaid reactivation, custody proceedings, parole requirements — the first 48 hours after release determine everything.
