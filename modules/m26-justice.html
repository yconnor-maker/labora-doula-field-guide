Justice-Involved Clients

Approximately 55,000 pregnant people are incarcerated in the US each year. Shackling during labor still occurs despite bans. Prenatal care is often inadequate. Baby separation at 24–72 hours is standard. Doulas working in jails and prisons are providing some of the most critical birth support in America.

Scope of PracticeYou follow facility rules. You do not interfere with custody proceedings. You provide emotional support, birth advocacy within facility constraints, and reentry resource connection.

Key Issues

  • Shackling: Federal law and most states prohibit restraints during labor and delivery. However, enforcement is inconsistent. Know your state's law. Document any violations.
  • Prenatal care: Often delayed, inconsistent, or inadequate. Medication continuity is frequently disrupted. Advocate for timely appointments.
  • Separation at birth: In most facilities, baby is removed within 24–72 hours. A few states have prison nursery programs (NY, IL, IN, OH, WA, SD, NE, WV). This separation is traumatic — for both mother and baby.
  • MAT access: Pregnant incarcerated people with OUD often have MAT (methadone/buprenorphine) discontinued upon intake — this is medically dangerous and associated with miscarriage, preterm labor, and fetal distress (Module 21).
  • Reentry: Housing, Medicaid reactivation, custody proceedings, parole requirements, substance use treatment, mental health care — the first 48 hours after release determine everything.
  • Mental health: Rates of PTSD, depression, and anxiety are dramatically elevated. History of trauma is nearly universal.
From the PhysicianI've delivered babies in hospitals where the mother was shackled to the bed during labor. I've had to call security to remove restraints so I could safely manage a shoulder dystocia. This is not hypothetical — this is happening in American hospitals. If you're working as a doula in a correctional setting, know the law, document everything, and understand that your presence may be the only non-punitive human contact she has during the most vulnerable experience of her life.

Reentry Support

Critical First 48 Hours After Release

  • → Medicaid reactivation — coverage may have been suspended, not terminated
  • → Housing placement — shelters, transitional housing, halfway houses
  • → Custody proceedings — connect with legal aid immediately
  • → MAT continuity — ensure prescription transfer from facility to community provider
  • → Parole/probation reporting — missed appointments can result in reincarceration

Test Your Understanding

1. Shackling during labor:

Federal and most state laws prohibit it, but enforcement varies. Shackling during labor is dangerous — it prevents position changes, delays emergency response, and is classified as cruel and unusual treatment.

2. Discontinuing MAT during incarceration for a pregnant person with OUD can cause:

Abrupt opioid withdrawal in pregnancy is medically dangerous — associated with miscarriage, preterm labor, and fetal distress. MAT should be continued throughout pregnancy, including during incarceration.

3. In most US facilities, baby separation after birth occurs at:

24–72 hours is standard. Only a few states have prison nursery programs. This forced separation has lasting effects on bonding, breastfeeding, and maternal mental health.

4. The most critical factor in successful reentry is:

The first 48 hours after release are the highest-risk window for relapse, reincarceration, and system failure. Having a plan for housing, insurance, medications, and legal requirements is critical.

5. The doula's role in a correctional setting is:

Your presence may be the only non-punitive human contact she has. Follow facility rules, advocate with words and documentation, and plan for reentry from the first prenatal visit.
