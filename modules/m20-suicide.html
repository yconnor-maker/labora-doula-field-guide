Maternal Suicide

Suicide is the second leading cause of death in the postpartum year. Among women who die by suicide perinatally, the majority had contact with a healthcare professional in the month before their death. Someone saw them. No one asked the right question.

Scope of PracticeYou are not a crisis counselor. But you may be the person she trusts most. Your role: ask directly about suicidal thoughts, take every disclosure seriously, and connect her to professional help immediately. Asking about suicide does not cause suicide.

What You'll Learn

  • Understand maternal suicide as a leading cause of maternal death
  • Recognize risk factors and warning signs specific to the perinatal period
  • Learn to ask about suicidal ideation directly and compassionately
  • Understand lethal means counseling basics
  • Know when and how to activate emergency intervention
  • Care for yourself after a client crisis

The Numbers

Maternal suicide accounts for approximately 5% of perinatal deaths in the US. Among maternal deaths occurring in the late postpartum period (43 days to 1 year), suicide and overdose together are the leading cause. The methods tend to be more violent than non-perinatal suicide, suggesting higher intent and less ambivalence.

Black and Indigenous women face unique barriers to mental health care — stigma, provider bias, lack of culturally competent therapists, and economic barriers. The intersection of racial trauma, perinatal mood disorders, and systemic failure creates compounding risk.

From the PhysicianThe postpartum period is uniquely dangerous for suicide for several reasons: hormonal upheaval, sleep deprivation as a biological stressor, identity transformation, isolation, and the gap between expectations and reality. Many women feel they cannot say "I regret this" or "I'm not happy" because motherhood is supposed to be joyful. That silence kills.

Risk Factors

  • Prior history of depression, anxiety, PTSD, or bipolar disorder
  • Prior suicide attempt
  • Family history of suicide
  • Perinatal mood disorder — especially untreated
  • Traumatic birth experience
  • Intimate partner violence
  • Social isolation — especially during postpartum
  • Substance use disorder
  • Pregnancy loss, NICU admission, or infant health crisis
  • Financial stress, housing instability, food insecurity
  • Access to lethal means (especially firearms)

Warning Signs

⚠ Immediate Concern — Act Now

  • Direct statements: "I want to die," "I can't do this anymore," "Everyone would be better off without me"
  • Indirect statements: "I just want it to stop," "I'm so tired of everything," "You'd be a better mom for this baby"
  • Giving away possessions or making arrangements "just in case"
  • Sudden calmness or resolution after a period of severe depression
  • Researching methods of suicide
  • Stockpiling medications
  • Withdrawing from all support — canceling visits, not answering calls
  • Writing letters or messages that sound like goodbyes

How to Ask

Research consistently shows that asking about suicide does NOT increase risk. It often provides relief — she's been carrying this alone.

Direct Questions — Use These Words

  • → "Sometimes when people feel overwhelmed like this, they have thoughts about not wanting to be here. Have you had thoughts like that?"
  • → "Have you thought about hurting yourself?"
  • → "Have you thought about suicide?"
  • → If yes: "Do you have a plan? Do you have access to [method]?"

If She Says Yes

1
Stay calm. Stay present.

"Thank you for telling me. I'm glad you told me. You're not alone in this."

2
Assess immediacy

"Do you have a plan right now? Do you have access to pills, weapons, or other means?"

3
Do not leave her alone

Stay with her. Call for help while you are with her, not after you leave.

4
Call 988 or her provider

988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (call or text). Her OB/midwife. Her therapist if she has one.

5
If imminent danger: call 911

If she has a plan, access to means, and intent — this is a psychiatric emergency.

6
Lethal means counseling

Can firearms be stored elsewhere? Can medications be secured? Reducing access to means saves lives.

Lethal Means Counseling

Reducing access to lethal means during a crisis is one of the most evidence-based suicide prevention strategies. Most suicidal crises are temporary — if a person survives the acute moment, the majority do not go on to die by suicide.

  • Firearms: Can they be temporarily stored with a friend, family member, or at a gun shop? Gun locks?
  • Medications: Can prescription medications be held by a partner or locked up?
  • The conversation: "I'm not asking you to get rid of anything permanently. I'm asking if, while you're feeling this way, we can reduce what's within reach."

After the Crisis — Caring for Yourself

Supporting a suicidal client is one of the most emotionally intense experiences you will have as a doula. You must process it.

  • Debrief with a supervisor, mentor, or therapist within 24–48 hours
  • Do not carry this alone — secondary traumatic stress is real
  • Know your limits: if ongoing suicide risk is present, professional mental health support must be the primary intervention, not doula support
  • It is okay to cry. It is okay to be scared. It means you care.

Test Your Understanding

1. Does asking about suicide increase the risk of suicide?

Decades of research confirm: asking about suicide does not increase risk. It reduces isolation and opens the conversation for help.

2. Among late postpartum deaths (43 days–1 year), the leading cause is:

Suicide and drug overdose are the leading causes of death in the late postpartum period — the time when most clinical surveillance has ended.

3. Your client says "I just want it all to stop." Your best response is:

Follow up directly on ambiguous statements. "I want it to stop" could mean many things — ask specifically about suicidal thoughts. Clarity saves lives.

4. The most effective immediate suicide prevention strategy is:

Means restriction is the most evidence-based immediate intervention. Most suicidal crises are temporary — if the person survives the acute moment, they typically do not go on to die by suicide.

5. A warning sign of imminent suicide risk is:

Sudden calmness after prolonged distress can indicate the person has made a decision and feels "relief." This is a critical warning sign that requires immediate assessment.
