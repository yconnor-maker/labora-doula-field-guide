Perinatal Mood & Anxiety Disorders

1 in 5 birthing people will experience a perinatal mood or anxiety disorder. It is the most common complication of pregnancy and childbirth — more common than gestational diabetes, preeclampsia, or preterm birth. And it is the most underdiagnosed.

Scope of PracticeYou are not a therapist. You do not diagnose or treat mental health conditions. You screen, you normalize, you listen without judgment, and you connect people to care. For many women, you are the first person who asks how they're really doing.

What You'll Learn

  • Understand the full spectrum of PMADs beyond "postpartum depression"
  • Recognize the difference between baby blues and clinical disorders
  • Screen using validated tools (PHQ-2, Edinburgh)
  • Identify perinatal anxiety, OCD, PTSD, and psychosis
  • Know the crisis signs that require immediate intervention
  • Navigate referrals and resources within doula scope

Beyond "Postpartum Depression"

The term "postpartum depression" has become the default label for all perinatal mental health issues — but it's only one condition on a spectrum. PMADs include:

  • Baby Blues (50–80% of new parents) — Mood swings, tearfulness, irritability in the first 2 weeks. Self-limiting. NOT a disorder.
  • Perinatal Depression (~15–20%) — Persistent sadness, hopelessness, loss of interest, guilt, sleep/appetite changes lasting >2 weeks
  • Perinatal Anxiety (~15–20%) — Excessive worry, racing thoughts, physical symptoms (chest tightness, GI distress), hypervigilance about baby
  • Perinatal OCD (~3–5%) — Intrusive, unwanted thoughts (often about harming the baby) with compulsive behaviors. The thoughts are ego-dystonic — she is horrified by them.
  • Birth Trauma / PTSD (~9%) — Re-experiencing the birth, avoidance, hyperarousal, nightmares. Can occur after any birth — not only "complicated" ones.
  • Postpartum Psychosis (~0.1–0.2%) — Psychiatric emergency. Hallucinations, delusions, disorganized behavior, rapid onset within first 2 weeks.
From the PhysicianThe most missed diagnosis in perinatal mental health is anxiety — not depression. Women who can't sleep even when the baby is sleeping, who check breathing obsessively, who have intrusive violent thoughts they're terrified to disclose. If I only screen for depression, I miss half the people who need help. Ask about worry, not just sadness.

Baby Blues vs. Clinical Disorder

The distinction matters because baby blues are self-resolving and clinical disorders require intervention:

  • Baby Blues: Onset day 2–3, resolves by day 10–14. Mood lability but functional. Can care for baby. Feels better with support and rest.
  • Clinical Disorder: Onset anytime pregnancy through 12 months postpartum. Persistent (>2 weeks). Impairs function. Does NOT resolve with rest alone.

If symptoms persist beyond 2 weeks or worsen at any point, this is no longer "baby blues."

Screening — What You Can Do

You don't need a formal clinical tool to screen. You need two questions and genuine curiosity:

The PHQ-2 (Adapted for Doula Use)

  • 1. "Over the last two weeks, have you felt down, depressed, or hopeless?"
  • 2. "Over the last two weeks, have you had little interest or pleasure in things you usually enjoy?"

Add anxiety screening:

Anxiety Questions

  • 3. "Do you have trouble sleeping even when the baby is sleeping?"
  • 4. "Do you have thoughts you can't get out of your head — worries or scary thoughts about the baby?"

Crisis Recognition

⚠ Immediate Escalation Required

  • Suicidal thoughts or statements ("I can't do this anymore," "Everyone would be better off without me")
  • Thoughts of harming the baby that feel comfortable or planned (distinguish from OCD intrusive thoughts which cause distress)
  • Hallucinations — hearing voices, seeing things others don't
  • Delusions — fixed false beliefs, paranoia, religious preoccupation out of character
  • Disorganized behavior — inability to care for self or baby, confusion, agitation
  • Rapid onset of symptoms within first 2 weeks postpartum (possible psychosis)
Postpartum PsychosisThis is a psychiatric emergency. It affects ~1–2 per 1,000 births. Onset is rapid — often within the first 72 hours to 2 weeks postpartum. Women with bipolar disorder or prior psychosis are at highest risk. If you see hallucinations, delusions, or severely disorganized behavior: call the provider or 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline) immediately. She should not be left alone with the baby until evaluated.

Perinatal OCD — The One Nobody Talks About

Perinatal OCD is characterized by intrusive, unwanted, horrifying thoughts — often about accidentally or deliberately harming the baby. The critical distinction: these thoughts cause extreme distress. The person does NOT want to act on them. They are ego-dystonic.

Common intrusive thoughts include: dropping the baby, the baby drowning in the bath, sexual thoughts about the baby, stabbing the baby. These thoughts are symptoms of a treatable disorder, not indicators of danger.

Your client may be terrified to disclose these thoughts. If she does, your response matters enormously:

What You Can Say

  • → "Thank you for telling me. These kinds of thoughts are actually really common and they don't mean you're a bad mom."
  • → "The fact that these thoughts scare you is actually a good sign — it means you don't want to act on them."
  • → "There are therapists who specialize in exactly this. Can I help you find one?"

Test Your Understanding

1. What is the most common complication of pregnancy and childbirth?

PMADs affect 1 in 5 birthing people — more common than any obstetric complication.

2. Baby blues typically resolve by:

Baby blues onset around day 2–3 and resolve by day 10–14. Symptoms persisting beyond 2 weeks should be evaluated as a clinical disorder.

3. A client tells you she keeps having thoughts about dropping the baby down the stairs and it terrifies her. This is most consistent with:

Intrusive thoughts that cause distress are hallmark OCD symptoms. The distress is the key — she is horrified by these thoughts. This is treatable and does not indicate danger to the baby.

4. Postpartum psychosis typically presents:

Psychosis onset is rapid — often within days. Women with bipolar disorder are at highest risk. This is a psychiatric emergency requiring immediate evaluation.

5. The most commonly MISSED perinatal mood disorder is:

Perinatal anxiety is as common as depression but far less screened for. Standard screening (Edinburgh, PHQ-9) focuses on depression. Asking about worry, sleep despite baby sleeping, and racing thoughts catches what depression screens miss.
