Teen Pregnancy

Adolescent consent, Title IX protections, age-gap screening, and the unique clinical and social vulnerabilities of the youngest birthing people. Teens under 15 have the highest maternal mortality rate of any age group. They deserve expert doula care, not judgment.

Scope of PracticeYou do not provide legal counsel on consent or mandatory reporting. You understand the unique needs of adolescent clients, provide age-appropriate support, and know the laws that protect them.

Clinical Risks

Adolescent pregnancy is higher risk than adult pregnancy across multiple domains:

  • Teens <15: Highest maternal mortality rate of any age group. Higher rates of preeclampsia, preterm birth, anemia, and cesarean delivery.
  • Teens 15–19: Elevated risk of preterm birth, low birth weight, and STIs affecting pregnancy.
  • Nutritional deficiency: Still growing herself — calcium, iron, and folate needs are amplified.
  • Cervical immaturity: Higher rates of cervical insufficiency and preterm delivery in very young teens.
  • PMADs: Adolescents are at higher risk for postpartum depression with less access to support.
From the PhysicianWhen I have a 14-year-old pregnant patient, I'm thinking about three things simultaneously: the medical risk (she's at the highest end of the morbidity spectrum), the social situation (who got her pregnant? is she safe?), and the developmental reality (she is still a child — she processes information differently, she needs concrete explanations, and she may not be able to project into the future the way an adult can). Your doula care for a teen needs to meet her where she IS developmentally.

Legal Protections

  • Medical consent: Minors who are pregnant can generally consent to their own prenatal care in most states without parental involvement. Know your state's law.
  • Title IX: Schools cannot discriminate against pregnant or parenting students. She has the right to: remain in school, access tutoring, take medical leave without penalty, and return after delivery.
  • Mandatory reporting: If the father is significantly older (varies by state — typically 3–5 year age gap for teens), providers are mandatory reporters. Doulas should know their state's mandated reporter status.
  • FERPA: Her pregnancy status is protected health information — school staff cannot share it.

Working with Adolescent Clients

Age-Appropriate Support

  • → Use concrete, visual explanations — abstract concepts are still developing
  • → Repeat information — she may need to hear it multiple times in different ways
  • → Don't talk down — she deserves respect even if she seems young
  • → Assess her support system — who's involved? who's safe? who's harmful?
  • → Screen for coercion — how old is the father? was the pregnancy consensual?
  • → Connect with school social worker for Title IX support
  • → Your consistent presence may be her only stable adult relationship

Test Your Understanding

1. Which age group has the highest maternal mortality rate?

Teens under 15 have the highest maternal mortality rate — higher than even the over-40 age group. They face the highest medical and social risk simultaneously.

2. Under Title IX, a pregnant student has the right to:

Title IX prohibits discrimination against pregnant students in all federally funded schools. She cannot be expelled, excluded, or penalized for pregnancy or parenting.

3. When working with a 14-year-old client, the most important communication adjustment is:

Adolescent brains are still developing prefrontal cortex function — abstract thinking, future projection, and risk assessment are not fully mature. Concrete examples, visual aids, and repetition are essential.

4. If a 13-year-old's partner is 22, you should:

A significant age gap with a minor is a statutory concern in every state. Know your state's mandatory reporting requirements and age-gap thresholds. This is not optional.

5. Adolescent pregnancies are nutritionally higher risk because:

She's growing a baby while still growing herself. Calcium, iron, and folate demands are higher. Bone health is particularly important — pregnancy in a growing skeleton can deplete calcium stores.
