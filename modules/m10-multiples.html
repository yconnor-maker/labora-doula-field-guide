Intermediate Tier · Module 10

Multiples

Twin and higher-order pregnancies carry unique risks — preterm labor, preeclampsia, growth discordance, postpartum hemorrhage, and the physical and emotional intensity of caring for multiple newborns simultaneously. Understanding the clinical landscape and the postpartum reality helps you provide the enhanced support these families need.

Scope of PracticeYou do not assess fetal growth, determine chorionicity, or make delivery timing recommendations. You understand the higher-risk profile, provide enhanced pregnancy and postpartum support, and know when the risks specific to multiples require escalation.

What You'll Learn

  • Understand chorionicity and why it matters for risk
  • Know the key pregnancy complications specific to multiples
  • Prepare families for NICU probability and delivery planning
  • Support the exponentially harder postpartum period
  • Recognize warning signs that are MORE likely in multiples

Chorionicity — Why It Matters

Not all twins are equal in risk. Chorionicity (number of placentas) determines the risk profile:

  • Dichorionic-Diamniotic (Di/Di): Two placentas, two sacs. All fraternal twins and ~30% of identical twins. Lowest risk category.
  • Monochorionic-Diamniotic (Mo/Di): One placenta, two sacs. ~70% of identical twins. Risk of twin-to-twin transfusion syndrome (TTTS), selective growth restriction. Requires closer surveillance.
  • Monochorionic-Monoamniotic (Mo/Mo): One placenta, one sac. Rarest and highest risk. Cord entanglement risk. Usually admitted for continuous monitoring from ~24–28 weeks with planned delivery at 32–34 weeks.
From the PhysicianWhen a client tells you she's having twins, the first question I want you to think about is: does she know her chorionicity? One placenta vs. two completely changes the pregnancy. If she doesn't know — or if she says "they share a placenta" — her surveillance schedule should be more frequent. Mo/Di twins need ultrasound every 2 weeks starting at 16 weeks to screen for TTTS. This is not optional.

Key Pregnancy Risks

  • Preterm birth: ~60% of twins deliver before 37 weeks. Average twin delivery: 36 weeks. Average triplet delivery: 32 weeks. NICU stay is probable for triplets and common for twins.
  • Preeclampsia: 2–3× higher risk. Larger placental mass → more placental dysfunction potential.
  • Gestational diabetes: Increased risk due to greater placental hormonal load.
  • Growth discordance: One twin growing significantly less than the other (>20% estimated weight difference). May indicate TTTS in monochorionic twins or placental insufficiency.
  • Postpartum hemorrhage: Overdistended uterus = higher risk of uterine atony after delivery (Module 15).
  • Cesarean delivery: Higher rate overall. Vaginal delivery of twins is often possible when Twin A is head-down, but malpresentation of the second twin after delivery of the first may necessitate cesarean for Twin B.

⚠ Warning Signs — Multiples-Specific

  • Rapid uterine growth — sudden increase in belly size may indicate polyhydramnios (TTTS)
  • Preterm contractions — regular tightening before 37 weeks (see Module 8)
  • Sudden weight gain >5 lbs in a week — fluid retention (preeclampsia)
  • One baby moving less than usual — fetal movement differences between twins warrant assessment
  • Cervical pressure or change — shortening cervix is more common in multiples

Delivery Planning

Help your client have a detailed birth preferences discussion with her provider. Key questions:

  • Is vaginal delivery being considered? What are the criteria?
  • What gestational age is the planned delivery? (Di/Di: 38 weeks. Mo/Di: 36 weeks. Mo/Mo: 32–34 weeks.)
  • Will there be two teams in the delivery room?
  • What is the plan if Twin B doesn't deliver vaginally after Twin A?
  • Will NICU be present at delivery?

The Postpartum Reality

The postpartum period with multiples is exponentially harder. Sleep deprivation is more severe, breastfeeding logistics are complex, financial strain is amplified, relationship stress intensifies, and the risk of PMADs is significantly higher. Your presence as a doula during this period may be the most valuable service you provide for any client.

What You Can Do

  • → Help coordinate feeding schedules — tandem feeding support, staggered vs. simultaneous
  • → Watch for PPH signs more closely — uterine overdistension = higher atony risk
  • → Screen for PMADs more frequently — risk is elevated in multiples
  • → Help with logistics: who holds baby A while feeding baby B
  • → NICU support: if one or both babies are in NICU, she is split between hospital and home. This is excruciating.
  • → Connect to Mothers of Multiples support groups (local chapters nationwide)

Test Your Understanding

1. Average gestational age at delivery for twins is:

Average twin delivery occurs at 36 weeks. Approximately 60% of twins deliver preterm (<37 weeks).

2. Twin-to-twin transfusion syndrome (TTTS) occurs in which type of twins?

TTTS occurs only in monochorionic twins — when shared placental blood vessel connections allow unequal blood distribution between the twins.

3. Why are multiples at higher risk for postpartum hemorrhage?

The uterus stretches more with multiples. After delivery, an overdistended uterus is more likely to fail to contract (atony) — the #1 cause of PPH.

4. Mo/Mo twins are highest risk because of:

Mo/Mo twins share one sac — the umbilical cords can entangle. This is why they're often admitted for continuous monitoring from ~24–28 weeks.

5. When one twin is in the NICU and one is home, the doula's most important role is:

Being split between NICU and home is excruciating. She feels guilty everywhere — when she's at the hospital, she's not with the home baby. When she's home, she's not with the NICU baby. Validate this impossibility.
