Basic Tier · Module 6

Common Interventions

Inductions, cesarean delivery, epidural anesthesia, operative vaginal delivery, episiotomy, and postpartum medications. You don't need to approve or discourage any intervention — you need to understand what's happening so you can help your client ask questions, process information, and exercise informed consent in real time.

Scope of PracticeYou do not recommend or discourage interventions. You help your client understand what is being proposed, ask questions, and exercise informed consent or refusal. You support her choice — not yours.

What You'll Learn

  • Understand the major obstetric interventions and why they're used
  • Know the common medications used in labor and postpartum
  • Help your client formulate questions before, during, and after interventions
  • Support recovery from operative delivery
  • Distinguish elective from medically indicated interventions

Induction of Labor

Labor induction starts contractions artificially when waiting for spontaneous labor poses risk. Methods include:

  • Cervical ripening: Misoprostol (oral/vaginal), Cervidil (dinoprostone insert), Foley balloon catheter, Cook balloon (double balloon)
  • Amniotomy: Breaking the membranes ("breaking the water") with an amniohook
  • Oxytocin (Pitocin): IV infusion, titrated up. The workhorse of induction.

Common indications: postdates (≥41 weeks), preeclampsia, PPROM, GDM with poor control, oligohydramnios, growth restriction, maternal request ≥39 weeks (ARRIVE trial).

From the PhysicianThe ARRIVE trial (2018) showed that elective induction at 39 weeks in first-time mothers did not increase cesarean rates and actually decreased hypertension. This changed practice. "Elective" induction is no longer a dirty word — when it's truly elective and truly 39+ weeks. Your client needs to know: induction is often SLOW (12–24+ hours from start to baby). Set expectations. Bring snacks. This is a marathon, not a sprint.

Cesarean Delivery

Surgical birth through an abdominal incision. ~32% of US births.

  • Planned/scheduled: Repeat cesarean, placenta previa, breech, maternal request
  • Unplanned/urgent: Failure to progress, non-reassuring fetal status
  • Emergency: Cord prolapse, uterine rupture, placental abruption — minutes matter

Recovery: Major abdominal surgery. 6–8 weeks of activity restrictions. Incision monitoring critical (Module 3). Driving restriction until she can brake safely (usually 2–3 weeks).

Gentle/family-centered cesarean options: Clear drape, skin-to-skin in OR, delayed cord clamping, music, narration of birth, lowering the drape at delivery. These should be discussed prenatally with the provider.

Epidural Anesthesia

Regional anesthesia placed in the epidural space. Provides pain relief from the waist down. The most common pain management method in US hospitals (~73% of vaginal births).

  • Works by: Blocking nerve transmission in the epidural space of the spinal column
  • Common side effects: Hypotension (most common — treated with IV fluids), urinary retention (catheter), pruritus (itching), shivering, headache (PDPH, ~1% — see Module 18)
  • Does NOT increase cesarean risk per current evidence
  • May slightly increase: Instrumental delivery rate, length of second stage, need for augmentation

Your role: Support her choice either way without judgment. Do not advocate for or against epidurals. If she chooses one — celebrate. If she chooses to labor without — support. Both are valid.

Operative Vaginal Delivery

  • Vacuum: Suction cup applied to fetal scalp. Used for prolonged second stage or non-reassuring fetal status. Risk: caput (swelling), cephalohematoma, rarely intracranial hemorrhage.
  • Forceps: Metal instruments guided around fetal head. Less common now. Risk: facial bruising, nerve injury (temporary), maternal laceration.

Both require informed consent and an experienced operator. Both have a "backup plan": if the instrument doesn't work, cesarean delivery follows immediately.

The B.R.A.I.N. Framework

Teach your client this framework for any intervention decision:

B.R.A.I.N. Questions

  • B Benefits: What are the benefits of this intervention?
  • R Risks: What are the risks?
  • A Alternatives: What are the alternatives?
  • I Intuition: What does my gut tell me?
  • N Nothing: What happens if we do nothing (or wait)?

Postpartum Medications

  • Oxytocin (Pitocin): Given after delivery to contract the uterus and prevent PPH. Most important postpartum medication.
  • Ibuprofen (Motrin) + Acetaminophen (Tylenol): First-line pain management. Alternating schedule. Safe with breastfeeding.
  • Stool softeners (Docusate/Colace): Prevent constipation and straining — especially important after perineal laceration or cesarean.
  • Iron supplements: If anemic from blood loss. Take with vitamin C for absorption. Can cause constipation.
  • Anticoagulants (Enoxaparin/Lovenox): After cesarean in high-risk patients to prevent DVT (Module 17).
  • Magnesium sulfate: Continued 24 hours postpartum for preeclampsia/seizure prevention (Module 14).

Test Your Understanding

1. The most common side effect of epidural anesthesia is:

Hypotension is the most common side effect — treated with IV fluids and position changes. Per current evidence, epidurals do NOT increase cesarean rates.

2. Postpartum oxytocin is given to:

Post-delivery oxytocin is the single most effective intervention for preventing PPH. It contracts the uterus to compress bleeding vessels at the placental site.

3. The ARRIVE trial showed that elective induction at 39 weeks in first-time mothers:

The ARRIVE trial (Grobman et al., NEJM 2018) showed no increase in cesarean rates with 39-week induction and decreased rates of hypertensive disorders.

4. In the B.R.A.I.N. framework, "N" stands for:

"What happens if we do nothing?" is one of the most powerful questions in obstetric decision-making. It reframes the choice and reveals whether urgency is real or perceived.

5. A gentle/family-centered cesarean may include:

Family-centered cesarean options should be discussed prenatally. Many hospitals now offer clear drapes, immediate skin-to-skin, and delayed clamping as standard options.
