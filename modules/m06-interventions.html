Basic Tier · Module 6

Common Interventions

Inductions, cesareans, epidurals, vacuum and forceps deliveries, episiotomy, and postpartum medications. Understanding what these interventions are, why they're used, and what your client will experience helps you support informed decision-making and reduce fear of the unknown.

Scope of PracticeYou do not recommend or discourage interventions. You help your client understand what is being proposed, ask questions, and exercise informed consent or refusal.

Induction of Labor

Labor induction starts labor artificially. Methods include: cervical ripening agents (misoprostol, Cervidil, Foley balloon), membrane sweeping, amniotomy (breaking the water), and IV oxytocin (Pitocin). Indications include: postdates (≥41 weeks), preeclampsia, PPROM, gestational diabetes with poor control, and maternal request at ≥39 weeks. Your role: help her understand the process is often slow (12–24+ hours), support her through early labor phases, and advocate for her comfort preferences.

Cesarean Delivery

Surgical birth through an abdominal incision. Planned (scheduled) or unplanned (during labor). Common indications: malpresentation (breech), prior cesarean, placenta previa, non-reassuring fetal status, failure to progress. Recovery: 6–8 weeks, activity restrictions, incision monitoring. Gentle/family-centered cesarean options: clear drape, skin-to-skin in OR, delayed cord clamping.

Epidural Anesthesia

Regional anesthesia placed in the epidural space of the spine. Provides pain relief from the waist down while the person remains awake. Side effects: hypotension (most common), urinary retention (catheter needed), pruritus (itching), shivering, headache (post-dural puncture, ~1%). Does NOT increase cesarean risk per current evidence. Your role: support her choice either way without judgment.

Postpartum Medications

  • Oxytocin: Given after delivery to contract the uterus and prevent hemorrhage
  • Ibuprofen/Acetaminophen: First-line pain management, safe with breastfeeding
  • Stool softeners: Docusate — prevent constipation and straining
  • Iron supplements: If anemic from blood loss
  • Anticoagulants: After cesarean in high-risk patients to prevent DVT

Test Your Understanding

1. The most common side effect of epidural anesthesia is:

Hypotension is the most common side effect — treated with IV fluids and position changes.

2. Why is oxytocin given after delivery?

Post-delivery oxytocin is the single most effective intervention for preventing postpartum hemorrhage.
