Hemorrhage — The Basics

This is your first exposure to postpartum bleeding assessment. Module 15 (Advanced) goes deeper into obstetric hemorrhage emergencies and the Four T's protocol. Here you learn what normal bleeding looks like, what's not normal, and when to pick up the phone — because the difference between "heavy period" and hemorrhage can be 15 minutes.

Scope of PracticeYou do not assess blood loss quantitatively or clinically. You observe pad saturation, clot size, and your client's symptoms — and you communicate what you see to the clinical team or provider. Your observation at home may be the only bleeding assessment happening.

What You'll Learn

  • Distinguish normal lochia from concerning bleeding
  • Use the pad count method and clot assessment
  • Recognize symptoms of significant blood loss (compensated shock)
  • Know the escalation triggers for postpartum bleeding
  • Understand why young, healthy people hide hemorrhage until they crash

Normal Lochia Progression

  • Days 1–3 (Lochia rubra): Heavy, bright red, may include small clots (<golf ball). Heaviest in the first 24 hours. Increased bleeding with breastfeeding (oxytocin → uterine contractions) is normal.
  • Days 4–10 (Lochia serosa): Pink-brown, decreasing volume. Occasional small red gushes with activity are common.
  • Days 11–28+ (Lochia alba): Yellowish-white, minimal discharge. Can last up to 6 weeks.

Activity increases bleeding temporarily — standing up after resting, breastfeeding, first bowel movement. Brief return to red bleeding is expected. Sustained heavy bleeding or return to heavy flow after it had lightened for days is concerning.

From the PhysicianHere's what I need you to understand about hemorrhage at home: young, healthy people compensate incredibly well. Their blood pressure stays normal and their heart rate only mildly elevates until they've lost 30–40% of their blood volume. Then they crash — suddenly, dramatically, and sometimes fatally. By the time she says "I feel dizzy," she may have already lost a liter. Watch the pads, watch the clots, watch her color. Don't wait for vital signs to be abnormal.

Pad Count Method

A practical way to assess bleeding at home. Teach this to every client before discharge:

  • Soaking 1 pad per hour for 2+ hours: Call provider
  • Clots larger than a golf ball: Call provider
  • Bleeding that had decreased then suddenly returns heavy: Call provider
  • Soaking a pad in <30 minutes: Call 911

Clot Assessment

Small clots in the first 24 hours are normal. Use common objects for size comparison:

  • Grape-sized: Normal in first 24 hours
  • Golf ball: Report to provider
  • Egg or larger: Emergency — call 911

Compensated Shock — The Silent Killer

This is the most important concept in this module. The body can lose 1–1.5 liters of blood before blood pressure drops. During that time, the body compensates by:

  • Increasing heart rate (earliest sign)
  • Constricting peripheral blood vessels (cool, clammy hands and feet)
  • Redirecting blood to vital organs (she looks pale)

⚠ Signs of Compensated Shock — Act Before the Crash

  • Heart rate increasing — she feels her heart racing
  • Cool, clammy extremities — hands and feet feel cold
  • Pale skin — especially around lips and nail beds
  • Anxiety, restlessness, "feeling off"
  • Excessive thirst
  • Lightheadedness, especially when standing
  • Weakness or difficulty standing
  • "Something doesn't feel right" — trust this

Escalation Pathway

1
Observe and count

Check pads — note time on each pad change (write it on the pad with a Sharpie if needed).

2
Assess her — not just the pad

How does she look? Color, alertness, skin temperature. "Are you feeling dizzy? Racing heart? Cold?"

3
Pad soaking ≥1/hr OR golf ball clots: call provider

"She is ___ days postpartum. She is soaking approximately 1 pad per hour / passing clots the size of ___. She [does/does not] have dizziness."

4
Compensated shock signs: call 911

If she is dizzy, clammy, pale, confused, or unable to stand — do NOT drive her. Call 911.

Case Scenario

You arrive for a postpartum visit on day 5. Your client had a vaginal delivery. She says her bleeding had lightened to pink by day 3, but yesterday it returned to bright red and heavy. She's been changing pads every 1–1.5 hours. She says she feels "kind of woozy" but blames it on not eating much. Her hands feel cool when you shake them.

ResponseBleeding that returns heavy after lightening + pad saturation every 1–1.5 hours + dizziness + cool extremities = possible hemorrhage with early compensated shock. Do not wait. Call the provider now. Say: "5 days postpartum, vaginal delivery. Bleeding returned heavy after resolution. Saturating pad every 1–1.5 hours. Lightheaded, cool extremities. Requesting evaluation today." If she worsens before provider responds — 911.

Test Your Understanding

1. What is the earliest clinical sign of hemorrhage?

Tachycardia is the earliest compensatory response. Blood pressure often remains normal until 30–40% of blood volume is lost. By then, she's in danger.

2. Your client is 3 days postpartum with pink lochia. She breastfeeds and notices a small gush of red blood. You should:

Brief red flow with breastfeeding is normal — oxytocin causes uterine contractions that expel residual blood. If it's brief and self-limiting, it's reassuring.

3. Which of the following is a sign of compensated shock?

Cool, clammy extremities indicate peripheral vasoconstriction — the body is shunting blood to vital organs. This happens BEFORE blood pressure drops. Note: normal BP during compensation is misleading, not reassuring.

4. At home, the threshold for calling the provider about postpartum bleeding is:

1 pad/hour for 2+ hours or golf-ball-sized clots = provider call. Soaking a pad in <30 minutes = call 911.

5. Why is "she looks fine" a dangerous assessment during postpartum hemorrhage?

Compensated shock is the most dangerous phase because it looks reassuring. Young, healthy bodies mask massive blood loss until sudden decompensation — which can be fatal within minutes.
