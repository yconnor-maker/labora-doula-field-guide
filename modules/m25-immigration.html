Connector Tier · Module 25

Immigration Barriers

Public charge fears, emergency Medicaid, language access, and serving undocumented clients. Immigration status should never determine whether a mother receives prenatal care — but it does. Fear of deportation prevents engagement with health services, leading to delayed prenatal care, emergency-only births, and preventable maternal deaths.

Scope of PracticeYou are not an immigration attorney. You do not ask about documentation status unless the client volunteers it. You understand available resources and connect without judgment.

Key Facts Every Doula Must Know

  • Emergency Medicaid covers labor and delivery regardless of immigration status in ALL states. She does not need to "show papers" for emergency services.
  • WIC does not ask about immigration status — available to all income-eligible pregnant, postpartum, and breastfeeding individuals and children under 5.
  • FQHCs (Federally Qualified Health Centers) provide prenatal care regardless of ability to pay or immigration status. Sliding scale fees.
  • Public charge rule: Using emergency Medicaid, WIC, SNAP, and CHIP generally does NOT affect immigration applications. This misinformation prevents millions from accessing services they're eligible for.
  • Language access: Hospitals receiving federal funds MUST provide qualified interpreter services — this is federal law (Title VI). Family members are NOT acceptable medical interpreters. A 5-year-old child should never interpret for her mother during a cervical exam.
  • EMTALA: Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act — any hospital with an ER must stabilize and treat emergency patients regardless of citizenship, legal status, or ability to pay. Labor IS an emergency under EMTALA.
From the PhysicianI've had patients arrive in advanced labor with zero prenatal care — not because they didn't want it, but because they were terrified that going to a clinic would result in deportation. They delivered babies with undiagnosed preeclampsia, uncontrolled gestational diabetes, breech presentations. Every one of those complications was preventable with basic prenatal care. Fear is the most dangerous barrier to maternal health in immigrant communities.

Your Role

What You Can Do

  • → Never ask about documentation status unless she brings it up
  • → Know your local FQHCs — names, locations, phone numbers, prenatal services
  • → Advocate for qualified interpreter services — refuse to use children or family as interpreters for medical decisions
  • → Proactively correct public charge misinformation: "Using WIC and emergency Medicaid does not affect your immigration case"
  • → Connect with local immigration legal aid for benefits eligibility questions
  • → Know your local community health workers (promotoras) — they are trusted liaisons
  • → If ICE concerns arise: know your client's rights. Hospitals are considered "sensitive locations."

Test Your Understanding

1. Emergency Medicaid covers labor and delivery for undocumented individuals in:

Emergency Medicaid covers labor and delivery in ALL states regardless of immigration status. This is federal law — no documentation is required for emergency services.

2. Under federal law (Title VI), hospitals must:

Title VI requires qualified interpreter services at any facility receiving federal funds. Family members — especially children — are not adequate medical interpreters. Advocate for professional services.

3. Using WIC, emergency Medicaid, and CHIP generally:

Public charge misinformation is one of the most damaging barriers. These programs are specifically excluded from public charge determinations. Correct this misinformation proactively.

4. FQHCs (Federally Qualified Health Centers) provide prenatal care:

FQHCs serve everyone regardless of status or ability to pay. They use a sliding fee scale. Every doula should know the FQHCs in their service area — names, numbers, and locations.

5. The most dangerous barrier to maternal health in immigrant communities is:

Fear prevents prenatal care engagement, leading to undiagnosed preeclampsia, GDM, breech presentation, and preventable deaths. Breaking through fear with accurate information is life-saving advocacy.
