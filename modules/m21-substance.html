Substance Use in Pregnancy

Addiction is a medical condition, not a moral failure. In 23 states, substance use during pregnancy can be prosecuted as child abuse. The criminalization of addiction drives women away from prenatal care, doula support, and the treatment that saves lives. Your role is to be the person she doesn't have to hide from.

Scope of PracticeYou do not provide addiction counseling or medication management. You reduce stigma, support engagement with care, understand harm reduction principles, and know your state's mandatory reporting laws.

What You'll Learn

  • Understand substance use disorder as a medical condition
  • Know the basics of medication-assisted treatment (MAT) in pregnancy
  • Understand neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS/NOWS)
  • Apply harm reduction principles
  • Navigate the legal landscape of substance use in pregnancy
  • Know when and how naloxone (Narcan) is used

The Landscape

Approximately 5–6% of pregnant people report using illicit drugs, and opioid use disorder in pregnancy has quadrupled in recent decades. Drug overdose is now the leading cause of pregnancy-associated death in several states. The opioid crisis has disproportionately affected rural communities, Appalachia, and Native American populations.

The most dangerous thing about substance use in pregnancy is not the substance — it is the avoidance of prenatal care caused by fear of prosecution, child removal, and judgment. Women who fear consequences delay or avoid care entirely.

From the PhysicianI have watched patients lie about substance use because they feared their baby would be taken. That fear is not irrational — in many states, a positive drug screen at delivery triggers a mandatory CPS report. But lying means we can't treat NAS properly, we can't optimize MAT dosing, and we can't prepare the NICU. The doula who creates a judgment-free space may be the reason she finally discloses.

Medication-Assisted Treatment (MAT)

MAT is the gold standard for opioid use disorder in pregnancy. ACOG recommends buprenorphine or methadone — NOT withdrawal ("detox") — during pregnancy. Abrupt opioid withdrawal in pregnancy can cause fetal distress, preterm labor, and fetal death.

  • Methadone: Full opioid agonist, dispensed at federally licensed clinics, requires daily visits. Effective but access is limited.
  • Buprenorphine (Subutex): Partial agonist, can be prescribed in office settings. Associated with less severe NAS than methadone.

Your client may face stigma from family, friends, and even healthcare providers for being "on drugs while pregnant." MAT is not "trading one addiction for another" — it is evidence-based treatment that reduces overdose death, preterm birth, and low birth weight.

Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome (NAS/NOWS)

Babies exposed to opioids in utero may develop withdrawal symptoms after birth — typically within 24–72 hours. Symptoms include: high-pitched crying, tremors, poor feeding, excessive sucking, sneezing, yawning, diarrhea, and sleep disturbance.

NAS is treatable. First-line treatment is non-pharmacologic: skin-to-skin, breastfeeding (if appropriate), low-stimulation environment, swaddling. If symptoms are severe, pharmacologic treatment (morphine or methadone) is used with gradual weaning.

What You Can Do

  • → Prepare the family: NAS is expected, treatable, and does NOT mean the baby is "addicted"
  • → Support rooming-in — babies do better with their mothers than in the NICU
  • → Encourage breastfeeding if the provider approves (usually safe with MAT)
  • → Advocate against judgmental language from staff — she is a mother, not a case

Harm Reduction Principles

Harm reduction meets people where they are. It does not require abstinence as a precondition for support.

  • Using less is better than using more
  • Using safer methods is better than unsafe ones
  • Staying in prenatal care is better than dropping out
  • Having naloxone available is a life-saving intervention, not an endorsement of use
  • Every positive interaction with a healthcare or support person builds trust

Naloxone (Narcan)

Naloxone reverses opioid overdose. It is available over-the-counter as a nasal spray. If you work with clients who use opioids, carry naloxone and know how to use it.

⚠ Signs of Opioid Overdose

  • Unresponsive to voice or touch
  • Slow, shallow, or absent breathing
  • Blue/gray lips or fingertips
  • Pinpoint pupils
  • Gurgling or snoring sounds

Response: Call 911 → Administer naloxone (1 spray in one nostril) → Begin rescue breathing if trained → Place in recovery position → Repeat naloxone in 2–3 minutes if no response.

Legal Landscape

Know your state's laws. In some states, prenatal substance use triggers mandatory CPS reporting. In others, it doesn't. ACOG opposes criminalization of substance use in pregnancy because it drives women away from care.

  • 23 states classify prenatal substance use as child abuse under civil law
  • 3 states classify it as a criminal offense
  • Mandatory reporting varies by state — know yours before you need to
  • If a client discloses substance use, your first job is support, not surveillance

Test Your Understanding

1. ACOG recommends treating opioid use disorder in pregnancy with:

MAT is the gold standard. Abrupt withdrawal in pregnancy can cause fetal distress, preterm labor, and fetal death. MAT reduces overdose death and improves outcomes.

2. NAS symptoms typically appear:

NAS symptoms typically emerge 24–72 hours after birth as the neonate clears opioids from their system.

3. The most dangerous consequence of criminalizing substance use in pregnancy is:

Fear of prosecution drives women away from the prenatal care, MAT, and support systems that improve outcomes. This is why ACOG opposes criminalization.

4. First-line treatment for NAS is:

Non-pharmacologic interventions are first-line: skin-to-skin, breastfeeding (if approved), rooming-in, low-stimulation environment, and swaddling. Pharmacologic treatment is added if symptoms are severe.

5. How should you respond when a postpartum nurse refers to your client as "the drug baby mom"?

Advocacy within the clinical setting is part of your role. Use person-first language, redirect stigmatizing labels, and reinforce her identity as a mother in treatment.
