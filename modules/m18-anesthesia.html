Anesthesia Complications

Epidurals, spinals, and general anesthesia save lives and relieve suffering — but they carry risks. Post-dural puncture headache, total spinal, failed intubation, and anesthesia-related maternal death. Understanding these complications helps you advocate and support in the OR.

Scope of PracticeYou do not manage anesthesia complications. You recognize post-procedure symptoms, support your client through them, and know when they're dangerous.

Post-Dural Puncture Headache (PDPH)

Occurs when the dural membrane is accidentally punctured during epidural placement (~1% of epidurals). CSF leaks through the hole. Severe headache that is WORSE when sitting/standing and BETTER when lying flat. Onset: 24–72 hours after epidural. Treatment: blood patch (anesthesiologist injects the patient's own blood into the epidural space to seal the hole). Conservative: bed rest, caffeine, hydration.

⚠ When Headache Is Dangerous

  • Headache with fever → meningitis
  • Headache with neurologic changes (weakness, vision loss, numbness) → subdural hematoma or stroke
  • Headache with severe hypertension → eclampsia (Module 14)
  • Any of these = emergency

High or Total Spinal

If anesthetic spreads too high, it can cause respiratory compromise, hypotension, and loss of consciousness. The anesthesia team manages this in the OR — your role is to stay calm and support the patient emotionally if she's awake and frightened.

Your Role in the OR

What You Can Do

  • → Stay near her head — talk to her, narrate calmly
  • → Watch for shaking (normal with spinal) and reassure her
  • → If she reports difficulty breathing: alert the anesthesiologist immediately
  • → Post-op: ask about headache — positional (worse sitting) = possible PDPH

Test Your Understanding

1. PDPH is characterized by a headache that is:

The positional nature of PDPH is the key diagnostic feature — worse upright, better lying flat. This distinguishes it from preeclampsia-related headache.
