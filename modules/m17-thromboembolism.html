Thromboembolism

Pregnancy increases clotting risk 5–10×. The postpartum period is the most dangerous window. DVT and pulmonary embolism account for approximately 9% of pregnancy-related deaths — and unlike hemorrhage, the onset can be subtle and easily dismissed.

Scope of PracticeYou do not diagnose DVT or PE. You recognize leg swelling, calf pain, and shortness of breath as potentially life-threatening and you escalate without delay.

What You'll Learn

  • Understand why pregnancy is a hypercoagulable state
  • Recognize DVT symptoms — especially the subtle ones
  • Know the signs of pulmonary embolism
  • Understand risk factors for peripartum VTE
  • Know when to escalate immediately

Why Pregnancy Creates Clots

Pregnancy activates Virchow's triad — the three conditions that promote clotting:

  • Hypercoagulability: Pregnancy increases clotting factors (fibrinogen, factors VII, VIII, X) and decreases natural anticoagulants
  • Venous stasis: The growing uterus compresses pelvic veins, slowing blood return from the legs
  • Vascular injury: Delivery — especially cesarean — causes endothelial damage

This is an evolutionary adaptation to prevent hemorrhage at delivery. But it creates a 5–10× increase in thromboembolism risk that persists for 6–12 weeks postpartum.

From the PhysicianThe highest risk period for PE is the first 1–2 weeks postpartum, especially after cesarean. But I've seen PEs at 5–6 weeks postpartum. If a postpartum woman has sudden shortness of breath, chest pain, or unexplained tachycardia — this is PE until proven otherwise. Do not wait.

DVT — Deep Vein Thrombosis

A blood clot in the deep veins, usually in the leg. Left leg is more commonly affected in pregnancy (due to uterine compression of the left iliac vein).

⚠ DVT Warning Signs

  • Unilateral leg swelling — one leg significantly more swollen than the other
  • Calf pain or tenderness, especially with walking or flexing the foot
  • Warmth and redness in the affected leg
  • Pain that worsens with standing or walking
  • Leg that "feels heavy" or "tight"

Important: Bilateral mild leg swelling is common in pregnancy. Unilateral swelling — one leg significantly larger than the other — is the key red flag.

PE — Pulmonary Embolism

A DVT that breaks free and travels to the lungs. PE can be rapidly fatal.

⚠ PE Warning Signs — This Is An Emergency

  • Sudden shortness of breath — cannot catch her breath
  • Chest pain — sharp, stabbing, worse with breathing
  • Rapid heart rate (tachycardia) at rest
  • Coughing up blood (hemoptysis)
  • Feeling faint or actually fainting
  • Anxiety, sense of doom
  • Low oxygen saturation on pulse oximeter

If You Suspect PE

  • → Call 911 immediately. This is a life-threatening emergency.
  • → Keep her upright (easier to breathe than lying flat)
  • → Do not let her walk or exert herself
  • → If she loses consciousness: begin CPR if trained

Risk Factors

  • Cesarean delivery (especially emergency cesarean)
  • Prolonged immobility (bed rest, long hospital stay, long travel)
  • Obesity (BMI ≥30)
  • Age >35
  • Prior VTE or family history of clotting disorders
  • Preeclampsia
  • Multiple gestation
  • Smoking
  • Thrombophilia (Factor V Leiden, Protein S/C deficiency)

Case Scenario

You're visiting your client 10 days after her cesarean delivery. She mentions her left calf has been sore for two days — she assumed it was from sitting in the rocking chair too long. You notice her left leg looks noticeably more swollen than the right. She says it's warm to the touch.

ResponseUnilateral calf pain + swelling + warmth at 10 days post-cesarean = DVT until proven otherwise. Call the provider today. She needs imaging (duplex ultrasound). Do not massage the leg — this could dislodge a clot. If she develops shortness of breath while you're there: call 911.

Test Your Understanding

1. Pregnancy increases thromboembolism risk by approximately:

Pregnancy creates a 5–10× increase in VTE risk through Virchow's triad: hypercoagulability, venous stasis, and vascular injury.

2. The most important distinguishing feature of DVT vs. normal pregnancy swelling is:

Bilateral swelling is common in pregnancy. Unilateral swelling — one leg significantly larger — with pain and warmth is the hallmark of DVT.

3. If you suspect PE, your FIRST action should be:

PE is a life-threatening emergency. Call 911. Keep her upright (easier breathing), do not let her exert herself, and stay with her.

4. A client mentions her left calf has been sore. You should NOT:

Never massage a leg with suspected DVT — this could dislodge a clot and cause PE. Assess for unilateral swelling and warmth, then recommend provider evaluation.

5. The highest risk period for postpartum PE is:

The first 1–2 weeks postpartum carry the highest PE risk, especially after cesarean delivery. But risk persists for 6–12 weeks postpartum.
