Bereavement & Loss

Stillbirth, miscarriage, TFMR, ectopic loss, and neonatal death. Being present in the worst moment of someone's life is the hardest thing you will ever do as a doula — and possibly the most meaningful. There are no perfect words. Your presence IS the intervention.

Scope of PracticeYou are not a grief counselor. You are present. You follow her lead. You do not impose timelines, religious frameworks, or silver linings on her grief. You hold space without trying to fill it.

What You'll Learn

  • Understand the types and prevalence of perinatal loss
  • Offer memory-making without pressure
  • Know what to say — and critically, what never to say
  • Support the physical recovery that accompanies loss
  • Recognize complicated grief and connect to professional support
  • Care for yourself after supporting a loss

Types of Perinatal Loss

  • Miscarriage: Loss before 20 weeks. Affects ~10–20% of known pregnancies. Often minimized by others ("it wasn't really a baby yet") — do not reinforce this. Her grief is valid regardless of gestational age.
  • Stillbirth: Death after 20 weeks gestation. Approximately 1 in 175 pregnancies. She will deliver her baby. She may hold her baby. This birth matters.
  • TFMR (Termination for Medical Reasons): A wanted pregnancy ended due to lethal fetal anomaly or severe maternal health risk. Grief + guilt + social silence + political stigma. Many families have no community to grieve with.
  • Neonatal death: Death within the first 28 days of life. Parents may be in NICU when it happens. Decisions about life support are agonizing.
  • Ectopic pregnancy: Implantation outside the uterus. Medical emergency AND loss simultaneously. Often treated with methotrexate (which prevents any pregnancy for 3+ months after) or surgery.
From the PhysicianI have told more women their baby has died than I can count. What I've learned: the words I use matter less than how I say them, and what I do after. Sit down. Be at eye level. Be direct — "I'm so sorry, there is no heartbeat." Then be quiet. Let her react. Don't rush. Don't explain why. In that moment, she doesn't need information. She needs a human being who is not afraid of her pain.

Memory-Making

Offer — don't push. Many parents who initially decline regret it later. Gently offer multiple times, across the hours you're together.

Memory-Making Options

  • → Holding the baby: Dress, wrap, hold. As long as she wants. There is no rush.
  • → Photography: Now I Lay Me Down to Sleep (NILMDTS.org) provides free professional photography for stillbirths.
  • → Handprints and footprints: Ink or clay impressions. Take multiple — they can be given to grandparents, siblings.
  • → Naming ceremony: Even if informal. Using the baby's name throughout validates their existence.
  • → Locks of hair, blanket, outfit: Keep these in a memory box. Hospital may provide one.
  • → Weight and length: Record them. They matter.

What to Say — And What Never to Say

✓ Say

  • → "I'm so sorry. I'm here."
  • → "Your baby mattered."
  • → Use the baby's name if they've chosen one.
  • → "There is no right way to grieve."
  • → "Would you like to hold her?" (offer repeatedly)
  • → "I don't know what to say. I'm just going to sit here with you."
  • → Nothing at all. Silence is acceptable. Your presence is enough.

⚠ NEVER Say

  • "Everything happens for a reason"
  • "At least you can try again"
  • "It wasn't meant to be"
  • "You're young, you'll have more"
  • "God needed another angel"
  • "At least you have your other children"
  • "It was probably for the best"
  • "I know how you feel" (you don't, unless you do — and even then, her grief is not yours)

Physical Recovery After Loss

The body doesn't know the baby died. It will still:

  • Produce milk — engorgement may occur 3–5 days after delivery. Options: cold compresses, tight sports bra, cabbage leaves, Sudafed. Binding.
  • Bleed — lochia follows the same timeline as a live birth
  • Experience hormone crash — the same estrogen/progesterone drop occurs, intensifying grief
  • Need a postpartum checkup — she should still be seen at 2–6 weeks

Caring for Yourself

Supporting a loss is secondary traumatic exposure. You will carry this.

  • Debrief within 24–48 hours with a supervisor, mentor, or therapist
  • Allow yourself to cry. This is not weakness.
  • Recognize you may feel guilt, helplessness, anger, or numbness — all normal
  • Take time before your next client visit if possible
  • Long-term: if you support losses regularly, ongoing clinical supervision is essential

Test Your Understanding

1. After a stillbirth, the body will:

The body doesn't know the baby died. Milk production, lochia, and hormonal shifts all proceed — adding physical pain to emotional devastation.

2. When a parent initially declines to hold their stillborn baby, you should:

Many parents who initially decline regret it. Gently offer multiple times — "Would you like to hold her? It's okay either way." They can't undo not holding, but they can choose to when ready.

3. TFMR is uniquely isolating because:

TFMR families carry compound grief: the loss of the wanted pregnancy AND the weight of the decision AND the political silence that prevents public mourning. They need you.

4. Which response to a miscarriage at 8 weeks is most appropriate?

Gestational age does not determine the legitimacy of grief. An 8-week miscarriage may represent years of trying, IVF cycles, or deeply held dreams. Validate without qualifying.

5. After supporting a bereavement client, the doula should:

Secondary traumatic stress is real. Debrief within 24–48 hours. Allow yourself to grieve. If you support losses regularly, ongoing supervision is essential — this work will break you if you don't process it.
