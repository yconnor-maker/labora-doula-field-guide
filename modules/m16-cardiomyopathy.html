Peripartum Cardiomyopathy

Heart failure that develops in the last month of pregnancy or within 5 months of delivery. It mimics normal pregnancy symptoms — shortness of breath, swelling, fatigue — which is exactly why it kills. Black women are at 3–4× higher risk.

Scope of PracticeYou do not diagnose heart failure. You recognize that shortness of breath, persistent cough, and inability to lie flat are NOT normal pregnancy symptoms and you escalate immediately.

What You'll Learn

  • Understand peripartum cardiomyopathy (PPCM) as a distinct entity
  • Recognize symptoms that mimic normal pregnancy but indicate heart failure
  • Know the racial disparities in PPCM outcomes
  • Understand the clinical workup and treatment
  • Support a client diagnosed with PPCM through recovery

What Is PPCM?

PPCM is a form of dilated cardiomyopathy — the heart muscle weakens and the heart enlarges, reducing its ability to pump blood effectively. It develops in the last month of pregnancy or within 5 months of delivery in a person with no prior heart disease. Incidence is approximately 1 in 1,000–4,000 pregnancies.

The challenge: every pregnant and postpartum person experiences some shortness of breath, some swelling, some fatigue. PPCM hides behind these "normal" symptoms until the heart is severely compromised.

From the PhysicianI have diagnosed PPCM at 2 AM in a woman who thought she just "couldn't catch her breath from carrying the baby upstairs." Her ejection fraction was 20% — normal is 55–70%. She was in fulminant heart failure. The symptoms she dismissed as normal pregnancy were: orthopnea (can't lie flat), paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea (waking up gasping), and persistent cough. If you hear these symptoms, please escalate.

Symptoms That Are NOT Normal

⚠ Escalate Immediately

  • Shortness of breath at rest or with minimal activity (not just climbing stairs)
  • Unable to lie flat — needs to sleep propped up on pillows (orthopnea)
  • Waking up gasping for air at night (paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea)
  • Persistent cough, especially at night or when lying down
  • Rapid, unexplained weight gain (fluid retention — >5 lbs in a week)
  • Heart racing or pounding at rest (palpitations)
  • Severe fatigue disproportionate to activity level
  • Swelling that is rapidly worsening and includes the face and hands
  • Dizziness, lightheadedness, or near-fainting

Racial Disparities

Black women develop PPCM at 3–4× the rate of white women and have worse outcomes — higher mortality, lower recovery of heart function, and more frequent need for mechanical support or transplant. Contributing factors include:

  • Later diagnosis — symptoms dismissed by providers as "normal"
  • Higher baseline rates of hypertension and preeclampsia
  • Weathering — chronic stress of racism as a cardiovascular stressor
  • Delayed access to cardiology specialty care
  • Genetic factors may play a role (research ongoing)

As a doula supporting Black clients: take complaints of shortness of breath, fatigue, and swelling seriously. Advocate for evaluation rather than reassurance.

What the Clinical Team Does

Diagnosis requires echocardiography showing reduced ejection fraction (<45%) without another identifiable cause. Treatment includes:

  • Heart failure medications: ACE inhibitors (postpartum only — contraindicated in pregnancy), beta-blockers, diuretics
  • Anticoagulation (PPCM increases clotting risk)
  • Monitoring with serial echocardiograms
  • Activity restriction in severe cases
  • In severe cases: mechanical circulatory support or heart transplant

Recovery: approximately 50–70% of women with PPCM recover normal heart function within 6–12 months. Those who don't may have chronic heart failure. Future pregnancy counseling is critical — recurrence risk is significant.

Your Role

What You Can Do

  • → Know these symptoms. Teach them to your client prenatally and postpartum.
  • → Ask about breathing at every postpartum visit: "Can you lie flat comfortably? Do you wake up gasping?"
  • → If she reports needing multiple pillows to sleep: call the provider today
  • → Support a diagnosed client with logistics: medication reminders, baby care while she rests, emotional processing
  • → If sudden severe shortness of breath or chest pain: call 911

Test Your Understanding

1. PPCM develops:

PPCM develops in the last month of pregnancy through 5 months postpartum, in a person with no prior heart disease. This timing window is the defining feature.

2. Black women develop PPCM at what rate compared to white women?

The 3–4× disparity reflects both biological and structural factors — including delayed diagnosis, higher hypertension rates, and the cardiovascular toll of chronic racism.

3. "Orthopnea" means:

Orthopnea = difficulty breathing when lying flat. She needs to prop up on pillows to sleep. This is a hallmark of heart failure, not normal pregnancy.

4. Approximately what percentage of women with PPCM recover normal heart function?

50–70% recover normal ejection fraction within 6–12 months. Those who don't may have chronic heart failure and need counseling about future pregnancy risk.

5. Your postpartum client says she can't lie flat anymore — she needs 3 pillows to sleep and still wakes up gasping. She thinks it's "just being out of shape." You should:

Orthopnea + paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea = heart failure until proven otherwise. This needs evaluation today. An echocardiogram can diagnose PPCM, and early treatment improves recovery.
