Hypertension in Pregnancy

Hypertensive disorders complicate 10–15% of all pregnancies and are the second leading cause of maternal death in the United States. This module teaches you to recognize the warning signs, understand what the numbers mean, and know when a headache is not just a headache. Module 14 (Advanced) goes deeper into the emergencies that kill — eclampsia, HELLP, and hypertensive crisis.

Scope of PracticeYou may take blood pressure if trained and equipped, but you do not interpret clinical values or make diagnoses. You recognize warning symptoms — headache, visual changes, epigastric pain — and escalate immediately. Your observation may be the earliest detection point.

What You'll Learn

  • Classify the hypertensive disorders of pregnancy
  • Know what blood pressure numbers mean and when they're dangerous
  • Recognize preeclampsia warning symptoms
  • Understand why postpartum hypertension catches everyone off guard
  • Use an escalation script for concerning symptoms
  • Take blood pressure accurately

Types of Hypertensive Disorders

  • Chronic hypertension: Pre-existing HTN diagnosed before 20 weeks or before pregnancy. Requires monitoring for superimposed preeclampsia.
  • Gestational hypertension: New HTN after 20 weeks without proteinuria or severe features. Progresses to preeclampsia in ~15–25% of cases.
  • Preeclampsia: HTN after 20 weeks + proteinuria OR severe features (organ damage). The defining condition of obstetric hypertensive disease.
  • Preeclampsia with severe features: BP ≥160/110, organ involvement, neurologic symptoms. Medical emergency.
  • Chronic HTN with superimposed preeclampsia: Worsening of pre-existing HTN with new features — the highest-risk category.
From the PhysicianThe most important thing I need doulas to understand: preeclampsia is a disease of the PLACENTA, not the blood pressure. The blood pressure is a symptom. The actual pathology is abnormal placentation causing systemic endothelial damage — which is why it can affect the kidneys, liver, brain, and blood system simultaneously. This is also why delivery is the only cure — you have to remove the placenta.

Understanding the Numbers

  • Normal: <120/80 mmHg
  • Elevated: 120–129/<80
  • Stage 1 HTN: 130–139/80–89
  • Stage 2 HTN: ≥140/90 — diagnostic threshold for gestational hypertension
  • Severe range: ≥160/110 — ACOG: treat within 30 minutes. This is where strokes happen.

Taking BP Accurately

Inaccurate BP measurement is a leading cause of missed diagnoses. Follow these steps:

1
Proper cuff size

Bladder should encircle 80% of the arm. Too-small cuff → falsely elevated readings. Most people need a large adult cuff.

2
Position

Seated, feet flat on floor, back supported. Arm at heart level on a flat surface. No crossed legs.

3
Rest first

5 minutes of quiet rest before measurement. No talking during measurement.

4
Bare arm

Cuff on skin, not over clothing. Center over brachial artery.

5
Repeat if high

If elevated, wait 5 minutes and repeat. If still elevated, report to provider.

Warning Symptoms of Preeclampsia

⚠ Urgent Warning Signs — Escalate Immediately

  • Blood pressure ≥160/110 on any reading
  • Severe headache not relieved by acetaminophen or rest
  • Visual changes — blurriness, spots, flashing lights, blind spots
  • Right upper quadrant or epigastric pain (liver involvement — HELLP)
  • Sudden swelling of face and hands (not just ankles)
  • Confusion or altered mental status
  • Shortness of breath or chest tightness
  • Decreased urine output

The Postpartum Trap

Most people — including many healthcare providers — assume preeclampsia is a pregnancy disease that disappears with delivery. It doesn't. Approximately 40% of eclamptic seizures occur postpartum. Blood pressure often peaks at 3–6 days postpartum. Many women are discharged at 24–48 hours with no blood pressure monitoring plan.

At your home visits, you may be the only person checking blood pressure and asking about headaches and visual changes in the critical postpartum window.

Escalation Script

1
Ask at every visit

"Any headaches? Vision changes? Upper belly pain? Swelling?" Make this routine.

2
If symptom present: take BP

If trained and equipped. Record the number and time.

3
Call provider with specifics

"She is ___ weeks pregnant / ___ days postpartum. Her BP is ___. She reports [headache/vision changes/RUQ pain]. These started ___ ago."

4
Severe symptoms: go to L&D

Don't wait for a callback. Go directly to L&D triage, not the ER. L&D has the mag sulfate, the monitoring, and the team.

5
If seizure: call 911

Turn on side, clear area, do NOT restrain. This is eclampsia. Ambulance, not car.

Case Scenario

You're visiting your client at 36 weeks for a prenatal session. She mentions she's had a headache for the last day that Tylenol hasn't helped. She says her rings feel tight and her face looks "puffy." When you take her blood pressure, it reads 148/96. She says her provider told her she had "borderline blood pressure" at her last appointment.

What do you do?

ResponseNew-onset headache + facial edema + BP 148/96 at 36 weeks in someone with "borderline" BP = preeclampsia until proven otherwise. Call the provider NOW. Do not minimize. If you can't reach the provider quickly, take her to L&D triage. She needs labs (CBC, metabolic panel, LDH, uric acid) and fetal monitoring. Document: "36 wks, HA x24hr unresponsive to acetaminophen, facial edema, BP 148/96. Prior 'borderline' BPs per pt."

Test Your Understanding

1. Preeclampsia is fundamentally a disease of:

Preeclampsia originates from abnormal placentation. The blood pressure elevation, proteinuria, and organ damage are downstream effects of placental dysfunction causing systemic endothelial injury.

2. ACOG recommends treating severe-range BP (≥160/110) within:

Delayed treatment of severe-range BP is associated with maternal stroke and death. 30 minutes is the ACOG standard.

3. When does blood pressure typically PEAK in the postpartum period?

BP peaks at 3–6 days postpartum — often after hospital discharge. This is why postpartum monitoring is critical and why doulas asking about headache/vision at home visits saves lives.

4. A common error in blood pressure measurement that gives falsely HIGH readings is:

A too-small cuff is the most common error — it artificially elevates readings. Most adults need a large adult cuff. Always check cuff size.

5. Your postpartum client has a headache, BP 165/105, and says she sees "sparkles." You should:

Severe-range BP + headache + visual changes = postpartum preeclampsia with severe features. This is a medical emergency. L&D triage — not ER, not wait-and-see.
