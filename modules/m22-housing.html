Housing & Food Insecurity

A pregnant person without stable housing is a pregnant person in crisis. Housing instability is associated with preterm birth, low birth weight, inadequate prenatal care, PMADs, and substance use. Food insecurity affects 1 in 4 pregnant people enrolled in WIC. These are not social problems separate from health — they ARE health problems.

Scope of PracticeYou do not provide housing or financial services. You assess social needs, know the resource landscape, connect without judgment, and understand that a client who cancels every appointment may not be "noncompliant" — she may have lost her housing.

What You'll Learn

  • Understand the direct health effects of housing and food instability on pregnancy
  • Know the major federal assistance programs and eligibility
  • Screen for social needs without being invasive
  • Build a local resource directory
  • Recognize when a client is in crisis even when she doesn't say it

Why This Matters Clinically

Social determinants of health account for 80% of health outcomes. Medical care accounts for 20%. You can have the best OB in the country — but if your client is sleeping in a car, the prenatal care won't overcome the biology of chronic stress.

  • Housing instability → elevated cortisol → preterm labor, preeclampsia, growth restriction
  • Food insecurity → poor nutrition → anemia, low birth weight, GDM management failure
  • Utility shut-offs → inability to store medications or food → treatment failure
  • Doubled-up housing → exposure to unsafe environments, IPV, infection
From the PhysicianI prescribe prenatal vitamins and iron. She can't take iron without food — it causes nausea on an empty stomach. She can't afford food. I prescribe blood pressure medication. She has no running water to take it with. I refer to the nutritionist for gestational diabetes management. She doesn't have a kitchen. Every medical recommendation assumes a stable environment. When the environment isn't stable, the medicine can't work. That's why doulas who connect to resources are doing clinical intervention.

How to Screen

Most clients won't volunteer instability. Use normalizing language:

Screening Questions

  • → "A lot of the families I work with have questions about food or housing. Is there anything I can help connect you with?"
  • → "Have you had any trouble getting enough food in the last month?"
  • → "Is your housing situation stable right now? Do you worry about losing your place?"
  • → "Have your utilities been shut off or threatened?"

Resources You Must Know

  • WIC: Supplemental nutrition program for pregnant/postpartum/breastfeeding individuals and children under 5. Does NOT ask about immigration status. Covers specific foods (milk, eggs, cereal, fruits, vegetables, peanut butter).
  • SNAP: Food assistance (formerly food stamps). Apply at local DSS. Pregnant individuals may qualify at higher income thresholds.
  • 2-1-1: National helpline connecting to local services — housing, food, utilities, healthcare, childcare. Free, confidential, 24/7.
  • Diaper banks: National Diaper Bank Network — diaperbanknetwork.org. 1 in 3 families report diaper need.
  • Emergency shelters: Know your local DV shelters AND family shelters (they are often different).
  • LIHEAP: Low Income Home Energy Assistance Program — utility bill assistance.
  • Feeding America: Food bank locator — feedingamerica.org

Building Your Local Resource Directory

Before you need it, build it. Every doula should have a working list of:

  • 3 local food pantries with hours and eligibility
  • The local WIC office phone number and enrollment process
  • 2 emergency shelters (DV + family) with intake procedures
  • The local DSS office for SNAP/Medicaid applications
  • 2-1-1 as the universal backup for everything you don't know
  • Local mutual aid networks (often found on social media)

Case Scenario

Your client is 32 weeks pregnant. She's missed her last 2 prenatal appointments. When you visit, she's at a new address — her sister's apartment. There are 6 people in a 2-bedroom unit. She says she's "staying here temporarily" after her landlord locked her out. She hasn't eaten today. Her GDM is uncontrolled — she can't do the diet plan without her own kitchen.

ResponseThis is a cascade failure driven by housing loss. Immediate steps: (1) 2-1-1 call for emergency housing assistance, (2) WIC enrollment if not already — can provide immediate supplemental food, (3) Contact her OB's office to explain the missed appointments and housing crisis — they need to know the context, (4) Local food pantry for same-day food, (5) LIHEAP for utility assistance at the sister's apartment if applicable. The GDM management plan needs to be adjusted to her current reality. Tell the care team.

Test Your Understanding

1. Social determinants of health account for approximately what percentage of health outcomes?

Social determinants (housing, food, income, environment, education) account for ~80% of health outcomes. Medical care accounts for ~20%.

2. WIC does NOT require:

WIC does not ask about immigration status. It's available to all income-eligible pregnant, postpartum, and breastfeeding people and children under 5.

3. The best way to screen for food insecurity is:

Normalizing language reduces stigma and increases honest disclosure. "A lot of families I work with" lets her know she's not alone and won't be judged.

4. A universal resource number that connects to local housing, food, and utility assistance is:

2-1-1 is the national helpline connecting to local services: housing, food, utilities, healthcare, childcare. Free, confidential, 24/7. Every doula should have this memorized.

5. When a client with GDM loses housing, the appropriate response is:

Medical plans must match the client's reality. The care team needs to know the context so they can adjust — a "diet plan" without a kitchen is not a plan. Connect AND inform.
