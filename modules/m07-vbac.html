VBAC — Vaginal Birth After Cesarean

Supporting a client through a trial of labor after cesarean (TOLAC) requires understanding the real risks, the real benefits, and the one emergency that changes everything: uterine rupture.

Scope of PracticeYou do not counsel patients on VBAC candidacy. You support informed decision-making, advocate for your client's birth plan, and know the signs of uterine rupture so you can alert the team immediately.

What You'll Learn

  • Understand who is a candidate for TOLAC and who is not
  • Explain the evidence on VBAC success rates and risks
  • Recognize the signs of uterine rupture during labor
  • Know what the surgical team does in a rupture emergency
  • Support your client's autonomy in VBAC decision-making
  • Navigate the reality that many hospitals do not offer TOLAC

VBAC by the Numbers

Approximately 60–80% of people who attempt TOLAC will have a successful vaginal birth. This is a high success rate — but it depends heavily on individual factors. The primary concern with TOLAC is uterine rupture: a complete separation of the prior cesarean scar during labor.

The risk of uterine rupture during TOLAC with a low transverse scar is approximately 0.5–0.7% (about 1 in 150–200 labors). This is not zero. But it is also not the catastrophic risk it is sometimes portrayed as. The key is having a facility equipped for emergency cesarean within 30 minutes.

From the PhysicianThe biggest barrier to VBAC in America is not medical — it's institutional. Many hospitals have banned TOLAC entirely because they cannot guarantee 24/7 anesthesia and surgical coverage. Your client's access to VBAC may depend more on her zip code than her medical history. This is an access and equity issue.

Who Is a Candidate?

Favorable Factors

  • One prior low transverse cesarean
  • Prior vaginal delivery (strongest predictor of success)
  • Spontaneous labor onset
  • No recurring indication for cesarean (e.g., first was for breech, not CPD)
  • Adequate pelvis, no major fetal macrosomia
  • Facility with 24/7 emergency cesarean capability

Contraindications to TOLAC

  • Prior classical (vertical) uterine incision
  • Prior uterine rupture
  • Prior T-shaped or inverted-T incision
  • Active contraindication to vaginal delivery
  • Facility without emergency surgical capability

⚠ Uterine Rupture — Know These Signs

  • Sudden, severe abdominal pain — different from contractions
  • Pain that does not resolve between contractions
  • Sudden cessation of contractions
  • Fetal heart rate abnormalities (prolonged deceleration, bradycardia)
  • Vaginal bleeding (may be absent — bleeding can be internal)
  • Change in abdominal contour or loss of fetal station
  • Maternal tachycardia, hypotension, or signs of shock

What Happens During a Uterine Rupture

Uterine rupture is a true surgical emergency. The team's response is immediate:

1
Recognition

Fetal heart rate tracing shows prolonged deceleration or bradycardia. Mother reports sudden pain. Clinical suspicion is raised.

2
Emergency cesarean called

Goal: delivery within minutes. Anesthesia, NICU, and surgical team mobilize simultaneously.

3
Stabilization

IV fluids, blood products, hemodynamic monitoring. Maternal stabilization is parallel to delivery.

4
Surgical repair or hysterectomy

Depending on extent of rupture and hemodynamic stability, the surgeon will repair the uterus or perform a hysterectomy.

Your Role as the VBAC Doula

What You Can Do

  • → Help your client gather evidence-based information before she makes her decision
  • → Support her right to attempt TOLAC — and her right to choose repeat cesarean
  • → Know which hospitals in your area offer TOLAC and which don't
  • → During labor: watch for sudden changes in pain pattern, fetal heart rate alarms, or maternal distress
  • → If emergency cesarean is called: stay calm, reassure, step back for the team, and be there after
  • → After delivery: debrief. Whether vaginal or surgical, the emotional processing matters.

Case Scenario

Your client is in active labor during a TOLAC. She's been progressing well — 7cm, contracting every 3 minutes. Suddenly she cries out: "Something tore. That felt different." The fetal monitor alarm sounds. Her nurse moves quickly to check the strip.

What are you thinking? What do you do?

ResponseThis presentation — sudden severe pain "different" from contractions + fetal heart rate changes — is the classic presentation of uterine rupture until proven otherwise. You do not diagnose this. But you stay alert, stay calm, and stay out of the way. The team is about to move fast. Your job: reassure your client ("The team is taking care of you right now"), support the partner, and be ready for an emergency cesarean. After delivery, she will need you more than ever.

Test Your Understanding

1. What is the approximate success rate of TOLAC?

TOLAC success rates are 60–80%, with prior vaginal delivery being the strongest predictor of success.

2. What is the approximate risk of uterine rupture during TOLAC with a low transverse scar?

The risk is approximately 0.5–0.7% (1 in 150–200 labors) with a low transverse scar. Classical incisions carry significantly higher risk.

3. Which is NOT a sign of uterine rupture?

Gradually increasing contractions are normal labor. Rupture presents with SUDDEN change — pain different from contractions, abrupt fetal heart rate changes, or loss of station.

4. The biggest barrier to VBAC access in the US is:

Many hospitals have banned TOLAC due to inability to guarantee 24/7 anesthesia and OR coverage. Access depends on geography more than medical factors — a health equity issue.

5. During an emergency cesarean for suspected rupture, the doula should:

Your presence matters. Stay calm, stay visible to your client, reassure the partner, and give the team space. Your client needs to see a calm face. Emotional support is your superpower in this moment.
