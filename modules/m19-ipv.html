IPV & Maternal Homicide

Homicide is the leading cause of death during pregnancy and the postpartum year in the United States. Not hemorrhage. Not preeclampsia. Not embolism. Homicide. And the perpetrator is almost always an intimate partner.

Scope of PracticeYou are not a therapist, social worker, or law enforcement officer. You are a trusted person in her home. You screen. You listen. You create safety. You connect her to resources. You never pressure a disclosure or force a plan.

What You'll Learn

  • Understand the scale of IPV as a maternal mortality driver
  • Recognize behavioral and physical warning signs
  • Learn trauma-informed screening approaches
  • Develop safety planning skills within doula scope
  • Know when and how to connect clients with resources
  • Protect yourself and maintain boundaries

The Numbers Nobody Talks About

A landmark 2021 study in Obstetrics & Gynecology found that homicide during pregnancy or within 42 days postpartum occurs at a rate of 3.62 per 100,000 live births — making it the leading cause of maternal mortality in the United States, exceeding hemorrhage, hypertensive disorders, and all medical causes combined.

Black women are disproportionately affected, with homicide rates 2–3× higher than white women. Women under 20 are at highest risk. The perinatal period is the most dangerous time in a woman's life for intimate partner violence — abuse often begins, escalates, or changes character during pregnancy.

IPV affects an estimated 1 in 6 pregnancies. Many women see their doula more frequently than their OB. You may be the person who notices what the clinic visits miss.

From the PhysicianI have had patients who disclosed to their doula what they never told me. The clinic is 15 minutes with a paper gown and a medical chart. A doula sits in her living room for 2 hours. She sees who else is in the house. She sees the body language. She builds trust over weeks. This is why the doula's role in IPV recognition is irreplaceable — you see the whole picture.

Warning Signs — What You Might See

No single sign confirms IPV. But patterns should raise your awareness and change how you create space for disclosure.

⚠ Behavioral and Environmental Indicators

  • Partner answers questions for her or won't leave the room
  • Client checks her phone nervously, appears anxious about time
  • Missed prenatal appointments or canceled doula visits with vague excuses
  • Unexplained injuries or injuries inconsistent with her explanation
  • Withdrawal from friends, family, or activities she previously enjoyed
  • Partner controls finances, transportation, or phone access
  • Fear-based language: "He'll be upset if..." or "I'm not allowed to..."
  • Escalation of partner's behavior during pregnancy — jealousy, accusations
  • History of emergency department visits for "accidents"
  • Flinching, hypervigilance, or extreme deference to partner

How to Screen — Trauma-Informed Approach

Screening is not interrogation. It is creating a safe moment where someone can disclose if they choose to. The most important things are: ask when she is alone, normalize the question, and accept whatever she says.

When to Screen

  • During prenatal visits — preferably when partner is not present
  • At the first postpartum home visit
  • Any time you notice warning signs or behavioral changes
  • Any time she asks to speak privately

Sample Language

What You Can Say

  • → "I ask all my clients this because it's so common — has anyone ever hurt you or made you feel unsafe?"
  • → "Pregnancy can sometimes change relationships. How are things at home?"
  • → "Is there anything happening at home that makes you worried about your safety or your baby's safety?"
  • → "If you ever need to talk about anything — anything at all — I'm here. And anything you tell me stays between us unless someone is in immediate danger."

If She Discloses

1
Believe her

"I believe you. Thank you for trusting me." Do not question, minimize, or express shock.

2
Affirm her

"This is not your fault. You don't deserve this." She may have been told otherwise for years.

3
Assess immediate danger

"Are you safe right now?" "Is he here?" "Do you feel safe going home today?"

4
Offer resources — don't push

Share the National DV Hotline number. Offer to help her make a safety plan. Let her lead.

5
Do NOT confront the partner

This can escalate danger immediately. Never contact the partner or involve them in the conversation.

6
Document carefully

Keep notes in a secure location the partner cannot access. Use your professional documentation system, not texts or shared devices.

ImportantShe may not leave. She may not be ready. She may deny it next visit. This is normal. Leaving an abuser is the most dangerous time — homicide risk increases 75% at separation. Your job is to be a consistent, safe presence. Plant the seed. Keep the door open. Don't judge the timeline.

Safety Planning Basics

A safety plan is a personalized, practical plan for how she can protect herself and her children. You can help her think through it, but she makes the decisions.

  • Escape bag: ID, birth certificates, medications, insurance cards, cash, phone charger — stored with a trusted person
  • Code word: A word she can text or say that means "I need help now" — agreed upon with a trusted contact
  • Safe places: Identify shelters, family members, or friends where she can go
  • Phone safety: Clear call history, use a private browser, consider a prepaid phone for hotline calls
  • Children: Schools and daycares should know who can and cannot pick up the children

Resources

National Resources

  • National Sexual Assault Hotline: 1-800-656-4673

Protecting Yourself

Supporting clients experiencing IPV is emotionally taxing. Secondary trauma is real.

  • Debrief with a supervisor, peer, or therapist — not on social media
  • Know your mandatory reporting laws (varies by state — know yours)
  • Never meet with a client if you feel unsafe in the home environment
  • Set boundaries: you are support, not rescue. You cannot save her — only she can decide when and how to leave

Test Your Understanding

1. What is the leading cause of maternal mortality in the United States?

Homicide is the leading cause of death during pregnancy and the postpartum year, at 3.62 per 100,000 live births — exceeding all medical causes combined.

2. When is homicide risk highest for a woman in an abusive relationship?

Homicide risk increases by approximately 75% at the time of separation. This is why pressuring someone to leave is dangerous — safety planning must come first.

3. If a client discloses IPV, your first response should be:

Believe. Affirm. Assess immediate safety. Offer resources. Never pressure, confront the partner, or make decisions for her.

4. When screening for IPV, you should:

Always screen privately. Normalize the question: "I ask all my clients this because it's so common..." Never screen with the partner present.

5. Your client denies IPV despite concerning signs. You should:

She may not be ready. She may be testing whether you're safe. Accept her answer without judgment, let her know the door is always open, and continue screening at future visits.
