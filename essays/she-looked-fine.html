She Looked Fine

Three words that appear in maternal death review after maternal death review. Three vignettes. Three women whose deaths were preceded by the same phrase: "But she looked fine."

scroll ↓
Vignette One

The Marathon Runner Who Didn't Bleed That Much

Aisha, 32. First baby. Uncomplicated vaginal delivery at 39 weeks. Second-degree laceration repaired. EBL documented as 350 mL — normal.

She looked fine.

She was an athlete. Resting heart rate of 52. Blood pressure usually ran 105/65. After delivery, her heart rate was 88. Her blood pressure was 100/60. The nurse charted: "stable vitals."

But Aisha's heart rate had jumped from 52 to 88 — a 70% increase. Her systolic dropped 5 points. She was pale but her dark skin masked the pallor. She said she felt "a little dizzy" but attributed it to the epidural wearing off.

At hour 3, her heart rate was 110. Her blood pressure was 95/58. She was still smiling. Still answering questions appropriately. Still holding her baby. She was in compensated shock.

At hour 4, she stood up to use the bathroom. She collapsed. Blood pressure: 72/40. Massive hemorrhage from a cervical laceration that was never identified. The EBL of 350 mL was the visible blood. The rest was pooling internally.

What Was Missed

Her baseline vitals were never compared to her pregnancy vitals. A heart rate of 88 looks "normal" — but for HER, it was tachycardia. A BP of 100/60 looks "fine" — but for HER, it was dropping. Compensated shock looks exactly like a tired new mom until the body can't compensate anymore.

What the Doula Could Have Caught

"Her heart rate is higher than usual — she usually runs in the 50s." "She says she's dizzy when she sits up." "She changed her pad twice in the last hour." Any one of those observations, communicated to the nurse, changes the trajectory.

She looked fine. She was dying.

Vignette Two

The Headache on Day Six

Maria, 28. Second baby. Uncomplicated cesarean for breech presentation. Discharged day 2 with normal blood pressures. No history of hypertension. No preeclampsia during pregnancy.

She looked fine.

Day 4: mild headache. She took Tylenol. Better. Day 5: headache returned. She took more Tylenol. Attributed it to sleep deprivation. Her mother told her: "That's just what happens after a baby." Day 6: worst headache of her life. She couldn't see clearly — "everything has sparkles." Her face was puffy. Her rings didn't fit.

She called her OB's office. The answering service said: "Take Tylenol and come in Monday."

She seized at 2 AM. Eclampsia. Blood pressure at the ER: 198/118. She survived. She was in the ICU for 4 days. Her baby was cared for by her mother while she was intubated.

What Was Missed

Postpartum preeclampsia can develop in women with NO hypertension during pregnancy. BP peaks at days 3–6 postpartum — after discharge. The answering service didn't ask about visual changes, swelling, or blood pressure. "Headache postpartum" was triaged as routine.

What the Doula Could Have Caught

A doula visit on day 3–5 with a blood pressure cuff would have caught the rising BP before it reached crisis. The question — "Does the headache get better when you lie down?" — would have identified this as non-positional (not PDPH). Headache + visual changes + swelling = ER, not Monday.

"40% of eclamptic seizures happen postpartum. Most happen after discharge. Most happen when no one is watching."

Vignette Three

The Shortness of Breath That Everyone Explained Away

Keisha, 35. Third baby. Vaginal delivery at 38 weeks. Black woman. History of mild chronic hypertension, controlled with labetalol.

She looked fine.

Week 2: she mentioned to her mother that she was "out of breath going up the stairs." Her mother said: "You just had a baby. You're out of shape." Week 3: she was sleeping propped up on three pillows because lying flat made her feel like she was drowning. She thought it was acid reflux. Week 4: her ankles were so swollen she couldn't wear shoes. She thought it was "normal postpartum swelling."

Week 5: she coughed up pink-tinged mucus. She went to urgent care. Chest X-ray showed pulmonary edema — fluid in her lungs. Echocardiogram: ejection fraction of 25% (normal >55%). Peripartum cardiomyopathy. Her heart was failing.

She was admitted to the cardiac ICU. She survived. She will be on heart failure medications for years. She cannot safely have another pregnancy.

What Was Missed

Every symptom of peripartum cardiomyopathy mimics normal pregnancy/postpartum complaints: shortness of breath, fatigue, swelling, orthopnea (can't lie flat). Black women are 3–4× more likely to develop PPCM. Her symptoms were attributed to deconditioning, weight, and "normal postpartum" for 5 weeks.

What the Doula Could Have Caught

"She can't lie flat without feeling like she's drowning" is not acid reflux. "She's out of breath walking up stairs at week 2" is not deconditioning. A doula who knows Module 16 asks: "Are you sleeping flat or propped up?" That single question — asked at a postpartum visit — could have moved the diagnosis from week 5 to week 2.

She looked fine. Everyone said so. And that's the phrase that kills.

The Lesson: Fine Is Not a Vital Sign

"She looked fine" is not an assessment. It is an assumption — and assumptions are where women die. Your job is not to accept "fine." Your job is to ask the questions that reveal what "fine" is hiding.

"Are you dizzy when you stand up?" catches compensated hemorrhage.

"Does the headache get better when you lie down?" catches preeclampsia vs. PDPH.

"Are you sleeping flat or propped up?" catches cardiomyopathy.

Three questions. Three lives. You learned them in this guide. Now use them.
