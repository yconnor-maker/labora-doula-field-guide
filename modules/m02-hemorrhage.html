Hemorrhage — The Basics

This is your first exposure to postpartum bleeding assessment. Module 15 (Advanced) goes deeper into obstetric hemorrhage emergencies. Here you learn what's normal, what's not, and when to pick up the phone.

Scope of PracticeYou do not assess blood loss quantitatively. You observe pad saturation, clot size, and your client's symptoms — and you communicate what you see.

What You'll Learn

  • Distinguish normal lochia from concerning bleeding
  • Understand the pad count method
  • Recognize symptoms of significant blood loss
  • Know the escalation triggers for postpartum bleeding

Normal Lochia Progression

  • Days 1–3 (Lochia rubra): Heavy, bright red, may include small clots. Heaviest in first 24 hours.
  • Days 4–10 (Lochia serosa): Pink-brown, decreasing volume
  • Days 11–28+ (Lochia alba): Yellowish-white, minimal

Activity increases bleeding temporarily. Brief return to red bleeding after rest is common. But sustained heavy bleeding or return to heavy flow after it had lightened is concerning.

Pad Count Method

A practical way to assess bleeding at home:

  • Soaking 1 pad per hour for 2+ hours → call provider
  • Clots larger than a golf ball → call provider
  • Bleeding that had decreased then suddenly returns heavy → call provider

⚠ Escalation Triggers

  • Soaking a pad in 1 hour or less
  • Clots larger than a golf ball
  • Dizziness, lightheadedness, racing heart
  • Feeling cold, clammy, or "not right"
  • Bleeding that returns heavy after lightening

Test Your Understanding

1. Normal lochia rubra (days 1-3) is:

Lochia rubra is heavy and red in the first few days — this is normal. It should progressively lighten.

2. At what point should your client call her provider about bleeding?

Pad saturation in 1 hour and large clots are escalation triggers. Don't wait for fainting — compensated shock can hide significant blood loss.
