Intermediate Tier · Module 12

Lactation Challenges

Supply issues, latch problems, mastitis, tongue-tie, engorgement, and the emotional weight of breastfeeding struggles. The doula's role is not to be a lactation consultant — it's to normalize difficulty, provide practical support, and know when to refer.

Scope of PracticeYou are not an IBCLC. You do not diagnose tongue-tie, assess milk transfer, or prescribe galactagogues. You support, normalize, troubleshoot basics, and refer to lactation professionals when needed.

Common Challenges

Engorgement

Days 3–5: milk "comes in" with breast fullness, firmness, warmth. Severe engorgement makes latch difficult. Warm compresses before feeding, cold after. Hand express to soften areola before latch (reverse pressure softening).

Latch Difficulty

Most common reason for early breastfeeding cessation. Signs of poor latch: pain throughout entire feed (not just initial seconds), clicking sounds, baby slipping off, no audible swallowing. IBCLC referral within 24–48 hours of persistent difficulty.

Low Supply Concerns

Most common fear — and most often unfounded. Adequate output: 6+ wet diapers/day by day 4, regaining birth weight by 2 weeks. True low supply causes: insufficient glandular tissue, hormonal issues (thyroid, PCOS), retained placenta, medications (pseudoephedrine).

Mastitis

Breast infection: red, warm, firm wedge + fever + flu-like symptoms. Continue breastfeeding (or pumping) — emptying the breast is treatment. Antibiotics if symptoms don't improve in 12–24 hours. (See Module 3.)

Tongue-Tie (Ankyloglossia)

Restricted lingual frenulum limiting tongue mobility. Can cause poor latch, nipple pain, inadequate milk transfer. Diagnosis and treatment (frenotomy) is done by a provider — often a pediatric dentist or ENT. Controversial: overdiagnosis is common.

Emotional Support

Breastfeeding difficulty carries enormous emotional weight — shame, guilt, grief, identity crisis. Your job: validate without pressuring. "Fed is best" AND "your breastfeeding goals matter" can coexist. Support her choice, whatever it is.

Test Your Understanding

1. When should you refer to an IBCLC?

Early IBCLC referral (within 24–48 hours of persistent difficulty) prevents cascade failure of breastfeeding.
