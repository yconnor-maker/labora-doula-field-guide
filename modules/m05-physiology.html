Postpartum Physiology

The postpartum body undergoes more rapid physiologic change than at any other time in adult life. Understanding what's normal — uterine involution, lochia progression, hormonal shifts, cardiovascular redistribution — helps you distinguish expected recovery from dangerous deviation. If you don't know what normal looks like, you can't recognize the abnormal that kills.

Scope of PracticeYou do not assess clinical recovery. You understand the normal trajectory so you can notice when something deviates from it. Your knowledge of normal physiology is the foundation of every red flag you'll learn in this guide.

What You'll Learn

  • Track uterine involution and fundal descent
  • Understand the hormonal crash and its effects
  • Know cardiovascular redistribution and why it matters
  • Distinguish normal postpartum symptoms from pathology
  • Understand the postpartum timeline: what happens when

Uterine Involution

The uterus shrinks from the size of a watermelon (~1,000g) to a pear (~60g) in 6 weeks. This is the most rapid organ remodeling in the human body.

  • Immediately after delivery: Fundus palpable at the umbilicus. Should feel firm ("like a grapefruit").
  • Days 1–10: Descends approximately one fingerbreadth per day below the umbilicus.
  • 2 weeks: Back in the pelvis, no longer palpable abdominally.
  • 6 weeks: Pre-pregnancy size.

Breastfeeding accelerates involution (oxytocin → uterine contractions). "Afterpains" — cramping with breastfeeding — are normal and often more intense with subsequent pregnancies.

From the PhysicianA uterus that is not involuting — staying large, boggy (soft), tender — is a warning sign. Possible causes: retained placental fragments, infection (endometritis), or subinvolution of the placental site (which can cause delayed hemorrhage at 2–4 weeks). If at your 1-week visit her uterus is still above the umbilicus, that's not normal. Escalate.

The Hormonal Crash

Estrogen and progesterone drop precipitously after placental delivery — the most dramatic hormonal shift in human physiology. This single event drives:

  • Baby blues (days 2–14): Mood lability, tearfulness, irritability, anxiety. Affects 50–80% of postpartum people. Self-limiting within 2 weeks.
  • Milk production: Prolactin rises as estrogen falls. Milk "comes in" days 3–5.
  • Night sweats: Estrogen withdrawal causes vasomotor instability. Normal for 1–2 weeks. Night sweats WITH fever = infection (Module 3).
  • Hair loss (telogen effluvium): 3–6 months postpartum. Dramatic but temporary — hair shifts from growth to shedding phase.
  • Libido changes: Decreased desire is common and multifactorial (hormones + sleep deprivation + body image + pain + touched-out).

⚠ When "Normal" Becomes Pathology

  • Baby blues lasting beyond 2 weeks → PMADs screening needed (Module 11)
  • Night sweats + fever → infection, not just hormones (Module 3)
  • Mood symptoms including intrusive thoughts of harm → psychiatric emergency (Module 11)
  • Uterus not shrinking by 2 weeks → retained products or infection
  • Lochia becoming foul-smelling → endometritis (Module 3)

Cardiovascular Changes

Blood volume increases 40–50% during pregnancy (~1.5L of extra blood). After delivery, this extra volume is redistributed and excreted:

  • Postpartum diuresis: Increased urination for 2–5 days as kidneys clear excess fluid
  • Autotransfusion: Blood that was perfusing the uterus returns to central circulation after placental delivery — this is why BP can spike postpartum even in previously normotensive women
  • Cardiac vulnerability: The autotransfusion can unmask or trigger cardiomyopathy in vulnerable hearts (Module 16)

This cardiovascular redistribution is why postpartum women are at increased risk for both hypertensive crisis (fluid overload → elevated BP) and thromboembolism (hypercoagulability + stasis → clots, Module 17).

The Postpartum Timeline

0-24h
Immediate Postpartum

Highest hemorrhage risk. Fundus at umbilicus. Lochia rubra (heavy, red). Shaking/chills common. First void important.

1-3d
Early Postpartum

Baby blues begin. Milk transitioning. Engorgement days 3-5. Afterpains with feeding. Diuresis beginning.

3-6d
Critical Window

BP peaks (preeclampsia risk). Wound infections declare. Endometritis onset. Milk comes in. This is when problems emerge — and often when no one is watching.

1-2wk
Transition

Lochia serosa. Uterus back in pelvis. Baby blues should resolve by day 14. DVT/PE risk ongoing.

2-6wk
Recovery

Lochia alba. Hair loss begins (month 3-6). PMADs may emerge or worsen. Postpartum visit at 6 weeks.

Test Your Understanding

1. The uterus should return to non-palpable abdominally by:

By 2 weeks, the uterus should be back in the pelvis. Still palpable above the pubic bone at 2+ weeks? Concern for retained products or subinvolution.

2. Baby blues last:

Baby blues are self-limiting within 2 weeks. Symptoms persisting beyond 2 weeks require PMADs screening (Module 11) — this is the distinguishing timeline.

3. Why can blood pressure spike AFTER delivery even in previously normotensive women?

Autotransfusion of ~500mL from the contracting uterus increases central blood volume. This can unmask hypertension or trigger cardiomyopathy. BP peaks at days 3-6 postpartum.

4. Night sweats postpartum are caused by:

Night sweats from estrogen withdrawal are normal for 1-2 weeks. But night sweats WITH fever = infection, not hormones. Always check temperature.

5. The "critical window" for postpartum complications is:

Days 3–6 are the gap: most women are home, BP is peaking, infections are declaring, and the next medical visit may not be for weeks. This is where doula vigilance saves lives.
