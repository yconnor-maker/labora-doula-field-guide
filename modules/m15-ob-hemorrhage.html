Obstetric Hemorrhage

Massive blood loss, uterine atony, the Four T's, DIC — and what the doula sees when every minute counts. You cannot stop the bleeding. But you can be the person who noticed it was getting worse before anyone else did.

Scope of Practice You do not treat hemorrhage. You do not diagnose the cause. You observe, you communicate what you see, and you escalate. Your documentation of timing and volume changes may be the most critical data the clinical team receives.

What You'll Learn

  • Distinguish normal postpartum bleeding from hemorrhage
  • Understand the Four T's — the clinical causes of PPH
  • Recognize the signs of compensated shock before obvious collapse
  • Know what the clinical team does during a hemorrhage emergency
  • Use a structured escalation script when bleeding gets worse
  • Document the timeline that the clinical team needs

Why This Module Matters

Postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) is the leading cause of maternal death worldwide. In the United States, hemorrhage accounts for approximately 11% of pregnancy-related deaths — and most experts believe many of these deaths are preventable with earlier recognition and faster response.

The clinical definition of PPH has evolved. Currently, ACOG defines PPH as cumulative blood loss ≥1,000 mL or any blood loss associated with signs of hypovolemia within 24 hours of delivery. Quantitative blood loss (QBL) measurement — weighing pads and sponges — is replacing visual estimation, which is notoriously inaccurate.

From the Physician All people bleed after birth. The challenge is distinguishing normal lochia from dangerous hemorrhage. Normal lochia progresses: moderate red → pink → brown → yellowish over 2–6 weeks. A return to bright red bleeding after it had lightened — especially with clots — is concerning. The body compensates for significant blood loss before showing obvious distress. Do not wait to see if it gets better.

The Four T's — Causes of PPH

The clinical team uses the "Four T's" framework to rapidly identify the cause of hemorrhage and direct treatment. As a doula, understanding this framework helps you communicate what you're observing in language the team recognizes.

1. Tone — Uterine Atony (Most Common — 70–80% of PPH)

The uterus should contract firmly after delivery to compress the blood vessels at the placental site. When it doesn't — when the uterus feels soft or "boggy" instead of firm — blood loss can be massive and rapid. Risk factors include prolonged labor, overdistension (twins, polyhydramnios, macrosomia), chorioamnionitis, and magnesium sulfate use.

2. Tissue — Retained Products

Retained placental fragments or blood clots prevent the uterus from contracting fully. The provider will examine the placenta after delivery to ensure it is complete. If fragments remain, manual exploration or surgical evacuation may be needed.

3. Trauma — Lacerations and Rupture

Cervical, vaginal, or perineal lacerations can cause significant bleeding even when the uterus is firm. Uterine rupture — a full-thickness disruption of the uterine wall — is a catastrophic emergency most associated with prior cesarean delivery. Signs include sudden severe abdominal pain, loss of fetal station, and hemodynamic instability.

4. Thrombin — Coagulopathy

Clotting disorders — whether pre-existing or acquired during labor — impair the body's ability to stop bleeding. Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) can develop during severe hemorrhage, placental abruption, amniotic fluid embolism, or sepsis.

What You'll See — The Doula's Perspective

⚠ Red Flags — Escalate Immediately

  • Soaking through a pad in 1 hour or less
  • Blood clots larger than a golf ball
  • Bleeding that was decreasing then suddenly becomes heavy again
  • Dizziness, lightheadedness, or near-fainting
  • Racing or pounding heartbeat at rest
  • Feeling cold, clammy, or looking very pale
  • Confusion, difficulty staying alert, or feeling "not right"
Compensated Shock — The Silent Danger A healthy postpartum person can lose 15–20% of their blood volume before their vital signs change noticeably. The body compensates by increasing heart rate and constricting blood vessels. By the time blood pressure drops, they may have already lost 30–40% of their blood volume. Watch for: increasing heart rate, anxiety or restlessness, pallor, cool extremities — these are early signs the team needs to hear about.

What the Clinical Team Does

Understanding the team's response helps you stay calm, anticipate needs, and know what's happening around your client. You are not performing these interventions — but you should recognize them.

Immediate Actions (Universal for All PPH)

1
Call for help

Charge nurse, OB backup, anesthesia, and blood bank are notified simultaneously.

2
Quantitative blood loss

Weighing pads and sponges begins. Every gram over dry weight = 1 mL of blood.

3
Fundal massage

The nurse or provider massages the top of the uterus to stimulate contraction.

4
Empty the bladder

A full bladder prevents the uterus from contracting. A catheter may be placed.

5
IV access and fluids

Large-bore IV (≥18 gauge) for rapid fluid and blood product administration.

6
Uterotonic medications

Oxytocin → Methergine → Hemabate → Misoprostol → Tranexamic acid (TXA). Sequential escalation.

Escalation — When Medications Don't Work

If bleeding continues despite 2–3 uterotonic medications, or blood loss exceeds 1,500 mL with ongoing instability, the team escalates to:

  • Uterine tamponade — A balloon (Bakri balloon) is placed inside the uterus to apply internal pressure
  • Uterine compression sutures — B-Lynch or modified sutures compress the uterus surgically
  • Uterine artery ligation — Surgical tying of arteries to reduce blood flow
  • Hysterectomy — Last resort. Removal of the uterus to save the patient's life

Massive Transfusion Protocol

When blood loss reaches ≥1,500 mL with ongoing hemorrhage, the massive transfusion protocol (MTP) is activated. Blood products are administered in a 1:1:1 ratio — red blood cells, plasma, and platelets — to replace what's being lost and maintain clotting function.

Your Role During a Hemorrhage

What You Can Do

  • → Stay calm. Your client will look to you for reassurance.
  • → Stay out of the way but stay visible. The team needs space; your client needs to see you.
  • → Talk to your client. Narrate calmly: "The team is helping you. They're giving you medicine to help your uterus contract."
  • → Support the partner. They may be terrified. Guide them to a place where they can see but not interfere.
  • → Document the timeline. Note when bleeding increased, when team arrived, what was communicated.
  • → If you are in the home postpartum: call 911. Do not drive. Keep her lying down.

Escalation Script — When Bleeding Gets Worse

Use this when helping your client call her provider, or when communicating with the clinical team:

1
Help client assess what she sees

Pad count per hour. Clot size (golf ball? larger?). Color change. Is it first week (heavy expected) or later (should be lightening)?

2
If any red flag: call provider NOW

"She is ___ days postpartum with heavy bleeding that is increasing. She is passing clots the size of ___. She feels ___." Be direct. Do not minimize.

3
If dizzy: do NOT leave her alone

Help her to seated or lying position. Someone else makes the call. Never let a symptomatic person drive.

4
Rapid bleeding + confusion: call 911

Do not drive to hospital. Keep her lying down with legs elevated. This is a medical emergency.

5
Go to L&D triage directly if needed

"She is ___ days postpartum with heavy bleeding. She needs evaluation now." Skip the ER — go to L&D.

6
Document the timeline

Timing of bleeding change, pad count, clot size, symptoms. This gives the clinical team critical assessment data.

Case Scenario

You are doing a 3-day postpartum home visit. Your client tells you her bleeding had been getting lighter — "just spotting yesterday" — but this morning she soaked through two pads in an hour and passed "a big clot, like a plum." She says she feels dizzy when she stands up. Her partner is at work. The baby is sleeping.

What do you do? Think through the escalation pathway before reading the answer below.

Response This is a same-day emergency. The return to heavy bleeding after lightening, clots larger than a golf ball, and orthostatic dizziness = provider call NOW. Do not wait. Help her sit or lie down. Call the provider with her. If the phone wait is long, go directly to L&D triage. She should not drive. Call her partner. You stay with the baby or go with her — whatever keeps everyone safest. Document: "Day 3 PP. Bleeding had decreased to spotting by day 2. Day 3: soaked 2 pads in 1 hour, passed clot ~plum size, dizzy on standing."

Test Your Understanding

1. What is the most common cause of postpartum hemorrhage?

Uterine atony — failure of the uterus to contract — accounts for 70–80% of PPH cases. The "T" for Tone is always evaluated first.

2. Your postpartum client reports dizziness when standing, a racing heart, and pale skin — but says she's "not bleeding that much." What should you suspect?

The body can compensate for 15–20% blood volume loss before vital signs change obviously. Tachycardia, pallor, and dizziness are early signs of significant hemorrhage even when visible bleeding seems moderate.

3. ACOG defines PPH as cumulative blood loss of:

Per ACOG Practice Bulletin #183: PPH = cumulative blood loss ≥1,000 mL OR any blood loss with signs/symptoms of hypovolemia within 24 hours of delivery.

4. Your client at a home visit is 5 days postpartum and passes a clot "bigger than a baseball." She feels fine. What do you do?

Clots larger than a golf ball are a red flag regardless of how the client feels. "Feeling fine" does not rule out significant blood loss — the body compensates. Call the provider today.

5. What does TXA (tranexamic acid) do in PPH management?

TXA is an antifibrinolytic — it prevents existing clots from breaking down. The WOMAN Trial (Lancet, 2017) showed it reduces death from bleeding when given within 3 hours of delivery. It is now part of standard PPH protocols.
