Anesthesia Complications

Epidurals, spinals, and general anesthesia save lives and relieve suffering — but they carry risks. Post-dural puncture headache, high/total spinal, failed intubation, aspiration, and anesthesia-related maternal death. Understanding these complications helps you advocate, support in the OR, and recognize post-procedure warning signs.

Scope of PracticeYou do not manage anesthesia complications. You recognize post-procedure symptoms, support your client through them, know when they're dangerous, and alert the team immediately when something is wrong.

Post-Dural Puncture Headache (PDPH)

Occurs when the dural membrane is accidentally punctured during epidural placement (~1% of epidurals). CSF leaks through the hole, causing low-pressure headache.

  • Key diagnostic feature: Headache WORSE when sitting/standing, BETTER when lying flat. This positional nature distinguishes it from preeclampsia headache (which is constant).
  • Onset: 24–72 hours after epidural
  • Treatment: Blood patch — anesthesiologist injects the patient's own blood into the epidural space to seal the hole. Definitive treatment. Success rate: ~90%.
  • Conservative management: Bed rest, caffeine (300mg/day), IV fluids, abdominal binder

⚠ When Postpartum Headache Is Dangerous

  • Headache with fever → meningitis (infection in the spinal fluid)
  • Headache with neurologic changes (weakness, vision loss, numbness, seizure) → subdural hematoma or stroke
  • Headache with severe hypertension → eclampsia (Module 14)
  • Headache that is constant (not positional) → more likely preeclampsia than PDPH
  • Any of these = emergency escalation
From the PhysicianHere's the clinical pearl: if she's 3 days postpartum with a terrible headache, the first question is — does it get better when she lies down? If yes → likely PDPH (needs blood patch). If no → may be preeclampsia (needs BP check, labs, and possible magnesium). One is an anesthesia complication. The other can kill her. The positional question sorts them. Teach it.

High or Total Spinal

If spinal anesthetic spreads too high, it blocks nerves that control breathing and blood pressure:

  • Progressive numbness rising toward the chest and arms
  • Difficulty breathing — "I can't breathe" or "my chest feels heavy"
  • Severe hypotension
  • In extreme cases: respiratory arrest and loss of consciousness

The anesthesia team manages this — your role: stay calm, stay near her head, alert the team immediately if she reports breathing difficulty or numbness in her hands/arms, and reassure her.

General Anesthesia Risks

Used for emergency cesareans when regional anesthesia fails or isn't possible. Higher risk than regional because:

  • Difficult/failed intubation: Pregnancy causes airway edema — intubation failure rates are higher in pregnant patients than general surgical patients
  • Aspiration: Pregnant patients have delayed gastric emptying — risk of vomiting and aspirating stomach contents during intubation. This is why NPO (nothing by mouth) guidelines exist in labor.
  • Awareness: Extremely rare but traumatic — patient is aware during surgery but paralyzed

Your Role in the OR

What You Can Do

  • → Stay near her head — talk to her, narrate calmly, maintain eye contact
  • → Shaking/shivering is COMMON with spinal anesthesia — reassure her it's normal
  • → Nausea is common — have a basin ready, alert anesthesia
  • → If she reports difficulty breathing or numbness in hands: alert anesthesiologist IMMEDIATELY
  • → Post-op: ask about headache quality and position-dependence over next 3 days
  • → If she had general anesthesia: she may have no memory of the birth. Help her process this.

Test Your Understanding

1. PDPH is characterized by a headache that is:

The positional nature is the key diagnostic feature. Worse upright, better flat = PDPH. Constant regardless of position = more likely preeclampsia. This single question guides the differential.

2. Treatment for PDPH is:

The blood patch is definitive — the anesthesiologist draws blood and injects it into the epidural space, forming a clot that seals the dural hole. ~90% success rate with single attempt.

3. If your client in the OR says "I can't feel my hands" and "my chest feels heavy," you should:

Numbness spreading to hands/arms + breathing difficulty = high spinal. This can progress to respiratory arrest. Alert the anesthesiologist IMMEDIATELY — seconds matter.

4. Why is general anesthesia higher risk in pregnant patients?

Pregnancy causes airway swelling and delayed stomach emptying — making intubation harder and aspiration (vomiting stomach contents into lungs) more likely. This is why regional anesthesia is preferred.

5. Shivering during/after spinal anesthesia is:

Shivering affects up to 55% of patients receiving spinal anesthesia — it's caused by thermoregulatory disruption, not infection or emergency. Warm blankets and reassurance are appropriate.
