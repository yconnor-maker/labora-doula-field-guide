Insurance & System Navigation

Medicaid, marketplace plans, postpartum coverage gaps, emergency Medicaid, and the appeals process. The US healthcare payment system is designed to confuse — and confusion kills. A client who doesn't understand her coverage may avoid the ER, skip medications, or miss postpartum appointments because she fears the bill. Your job is to help her navigate.

Scope of PracticeYou do not provide insurance advice or legal counsel. You understand the coverage landscape, connect clients with navigators and enrollment specialists, and know the key facts that keep her from falling through the gaps.

The Coverage Landscape

Medicaid

Covers ~42% of all births in the US. Income-based eligibility varies by state. Covers prenatal care, labor and delivery, and — critically — the postpartum period.

  • The 60-Day Gap (historical): Until recently, Medicaid coverage ended 60 days postpartum — the most dangerous period for maternal morbidity and mortality.
  • 12-Month Extension: The American Rescue Plan (2021) allowed states to extend postpartum Medicaid to 12 months. As of 2024, 46 states + DC have adopted this. Know your state's status.
  • Emergency Medicaid: Covers labor and delivery regardless of immigration status in all states. No waiting period for emergency services.
  • Administrative churn: Many people lose Medicaid not because they're ineligible, but because they miss a renewal form. Help with paperwork.

Marketplace (ACA) Plans

  • Pregnancy is a qualifying life event — allows enrollment outside open enrollment
  • Navigators available at healthcare.gov (free, in-person or phone)
  • Subsidies available based on income
  • Cannot deny coverage or charge more for pre-existing conditions (including pregnancy)

CHIP

Children's Health Insurance Program. Covers children in families above Medicaid income thresholds but below marketplace affordability. Some states extend CHIP to pregnant individuals.

From the PhysicianI have patients who leave the hospital against medical advice because they're afraid of the bill. I have patients who don't fill their blood pressure medication because the pharmacy told them it costs $47. I have patients who skip their 6-week postpartum appointment because their Medicaid expired at 60 days. Every one of those is a system failure, not a patient failure. When you help a client understand her coverage, keep her enrolled, or connect her to a navigator — you are preventing medical harm.

What You Can Do

Key Actions

  • → Ask during pregnancy: "Do you have insurance? Do you know when it expires?"
  • → Know if your state has adopted the 12-month postpartum Medicaid extension
  • → Help her find a marketplace navigator at healthcare.gov
  • → Remind her about Medicaid renewal paperwork — set phone reminders
  • → Connect undocumented clients with emergency Medicaid and FQHCs (Module 25)
  • → Know that hospital financial counselors exist — connect before discharge
  • → Never assume she has coverage — always ask

Test Your Understanding

1. Medicaid covers approximately what percentage of US births?

Medicaid covers ~42% of all US births — making it the single largest payer for maternity care in America.

2. Emergency Medicaid covers labor and delivery:

Emergency Medicaid covers labor and delivery in all states regardless of immigration status. This is federal law. No documentation is required for emergency services.

3. Pregnancy is considered a _____ for marketplace enrollment:

Pregnancy is a qualifying life event (QLE) — she can enroll in a marketplace plan outside of open enrollment. The ACA also prohibits denial or surcharges based on pregnancy.

4. The most common reason people lose Medicaid is:

Administrative churn — missing a form, not receiving the renewal notice, or not responding in time — is the #1 cause of Medicaid loss. Help her track renewal dates and complete paperwork.

5. How many states have adopted the 12-month postpartum Medicaid extension as of 2024?

46 states + DC have adopted the 12-month postpartum Medicaid extension. Know your state's status — the remaining states still drop coverage at 60 days.
